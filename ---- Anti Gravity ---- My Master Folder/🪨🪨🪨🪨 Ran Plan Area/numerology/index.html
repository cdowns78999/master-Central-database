Four questions worth sitting with.

01

Why do young kids feel drawn to numbers before they can even read?

The Number Sense: How the Mind Creates Mathematics

Stanislas Dehaene · Professor of Experimental Cognitive Psychology, Collège de France; Director, NeuroSpin Cognitive Neuroimaging Unit

“Children are born with an innate number sense — the brain is pre-wired to grasp quantity before language.”

Source: Oxford University Press

02

What does the academic world actually say about numerology?

The Mystery of Numbers

Annemarie Schimmel · Harvard Professor of Indo-Muslim Culture (1967–1992); scholar of Islamic mysticism and number symbolism

“Numbers carry layered symbolic meaning across every major culture — myth, religion, and science all weave through them.”

Top 5 reputable voices in this space
  1. Annemarie Schimmel · Harvard; The Mystery of Numbers — definitive cross-cultural study
  2. Stanislas Dehaene · Collège de France; cognitive neuroscience of number meaning
  3. Underwood Dudley · DePauw Math Emeritus; Numerology: Or, What Pythagoras Wrought
  4. Thomas Crump · U. of Amsterdam; The Anthropology of Numbers (Cambridge UP)
  5. Brendan McMahon · NIH/NIMH; peer-reviewed research on number salience across cultures

Source: Oxford University Press

03

Why did Joel Schumacher make The Number 23 — and what drew him to numbers?

“The Number 23” is a summation of Joel Schumacher’s filmography

Joel Schumacher · Director of The Number 23 (2007); also The Lost Boys, Falling Down, Batman Forever, Tigerland

Self-described “numerology agnostic” — Schumacher was drawn to paranoia and hidden oppression, not numbers themselves. Jim Carrey was the one fixated on 23; he’d renamed his production company JC23 before even reading the script.

Source: The Spool

04

When does fascination with number patterns become a mental health concern?

When the human tendency to detect patterns goes too far

Colin G. DeYoung, PhD · Professor of Psychology, University of Minnesota; Director, DeYoung Personality Lab

“Apophenia — seeing patterns that aren’t there — is the same mechanism that, pushed further, drives schizophrenic delusions like John Nash’s.”

Source: Psyche (Aeon Media)
