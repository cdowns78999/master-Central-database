Randy Engineerski — Engineer & Builder

Randy Engineerski is a systems and mechanical engineer with over a decade of experience designing, stress-testing, and deploying solutions that operate reliably under real-world conditions. He treats every constraint as a design parameter, not an obstacle.

From load-bearing structural assemblies to automated control systems, Randy's work lives at the intersection of physics and precision. If it moves, carries load, transfers energy, or needs to not fail at 3 AM — he's built something like it.

He believes engineering is fundamentally about accountability to the end state: a thing either works, or it doesn't. Randy builds things that work.

Based in the Greater Industrial Midwest, Randy collaborates with manufacturing teams, startups scaling hardware, and infrastructure partners who need engineering judgment alongside technical execution.

Skills & Technical Competencies

Randy's core toolkit spans mechanical design, systems integration, and failure-mode analysis. He's fluent in CAD modeling (SolidWorks, AutoCAD), finite element analysis, and tolerance stack-up — tools he uses daily, not occasionally.

On the controls side, Randy has hands-on experience with PLC programming, sensor calibration, and closed-loop feedback systems. He understands both the hardware and the signal — which means he can debug at the seam where most problems actually live.

Material selection, thermal analysis, and fatigue life estimation round out his analytical skillset. He's comfortable translating simulation results into actionable design decisions rather than just producing reports.

Randy also brings strong documentation and communication habits to every project — because the best engineered system is still a liability if no one can maintain it or understand why it was built the way it was.

Projects

One of Randy's flagship builds is a modular conveyor framework designed for high-throughput warehouse environments. The system operates at 99.4% uptime across three client deployments and was engineered for sub-2-hour field serviceability by non-specialist technicians.

He led the structural re-design of a 40-foot industrial press frame that was experiencing fatigue cracking under cyclic load. Root cause was traced to a resonance issue in the original design — Randy's redesign added dynamic damping and eliminated the failure mode entirely.

Randy has also developed custom fixture tooling for precision machined components, reducing part positioning time by 60% and eliminating a class of inspection rejects that had been attributed to operator variability but were actually fixture-induced.

Engineering Approach

Randy's approach starts with constraints, not concepts. Before sketching a single line in CAD, he maps load paths, environmental conditions, service life requirements, and failure consequences. The design space is defined by physics first, preference second.

He's an advocate for building physical prototypes early and breaking them on purpose. Controlled failure teaches more than any simulation — and it surfaces assumptions that look solid on paper but don't survive contact with reality.

Collaboration is built into Randy's process, not bolted on at the end. He works closely with machinists, fabricators, and operators throughout a project because the people closest to the hardware consistently hold knowledge that doesn't exist anywhere in the spec sheet.

Documentation, version control, and design rationale aren't afterthoughts — they're part of the deliverable. A design that can't be understood, maintained, or improved by the next engineer isn't a finished design.
