Act III

The Confession

A life in six chambers and one locked door.

Seraphine Mourn was not born with that name. She took it at seventeen, the evening she walked out of a cathedral choir in Vienna and did not look back. She will not say which cathedral, and she has asked us not to ask again.

What she will say is this: there was, in the house she grew up in, a pianoforte with one dead key. She learned to write around it. Every song she has written since — and there have been, by her count, one hundred and forty — contains a held breath where that missing note should have been. She calls it the silence that taught her to sing.

"When she steps on stage the lights seem to bow, not brighten. She sings as if the room were a chapel and she the last worshipper left." The London Review · 2025

Chamber I · Origin

A Childhood Built of Velvet and Weather

She grew up in a house her grandmother called the instrument — a narrow townhouse in a city she does not name, where the walls were padded with velvet and the windows did not open. Her mother collected tarnished silver. Her father conducted a community orchestra that never played. The first song Seraphine remembers writing was called What the House Says When No One Is Home, and it was composed on a dead key.

By twelve she was playing salon recitals. By fifteen she had been quietly asked to leave three conservatories, not for lack of talent but for what one instructor called "an insistence on feeling the music in the wrong places." She took the criticism as a compliment and left Europe shortly thereafter.

"Every record she releases is a black envelope slid under the door. You are not certain you were supposed to open it, and yet." NME · 2024
·  Chapter Second  ·
Chamber II · Exile

The Years of Small Rooms

From nineteen to twenty-four Seraphine lived in a series of small rooms — above a bakery in Lisbon, beside a taxidermist's shop in Edinburgh, in the converted attic of a former convent in Montréal. She recorded what would become her first EP, Candlework, on a portable tape machine her then-producer had smuggled out of a defunct Prague radio station. It was released without a single press photograph and sold out its run of four hundred cassettes in eleven days.

She has never toured a proper circuit. She performs, when she performs, in deconsecrated churches, abandoned ballrooms, and the occasional private library. Tickets are mailed, not scanned. The audience is never told the set list. The audience, as a rule, does not applaud until twenty seconds after the last note.

·  Chapter Third  ·
Chamber III · Vesper Hours

The Record at Hand

Vesper Hours, her forthcoming full-length and the third chapter in what she describes as "an ongoing liturgy of one," was recorded across four winters in a single room of a house she rents outside Prague. The room contains a piano, a cello, a single candle, and nothing else. She has said that any song which cannot be finished by the candle's last inch is a song that does not want to exist.

Eleven of the twelve songs on Vesper Hours were finished by candlelight. The twelfth, she will not discuss.

"She is the rarest kind of modern artist — one who seems unhurried by the century." Pitchfork · 2025

A Fourteen-Year Discipline

The making of a voice, in four movements.

MMXII

Early Lessons

Private Instruction · Age 11

First formal voice lessons under Madame Eleanora Vantu — weekly sonatine, nightly solfège, a single dead-key pianoforte.

MMXV · MMXIX

The Conservatory

Vienna Conservatoire · B.Mus., Voice

Four seasons of classical training in mezzo-soprano repertoire — Bellini, Bizet, Puccini, Rossini — graduated with distinction.

MMXX · MMXXII

Masterclasses

Salzburg · Prague · Paris

Studied under three living maestros; awarded the Lucrezia Bori Prize for interpretation. Recorded her first aria cycle in a disused chapel outside Prague.

MMXXIII

Professional Debut

Deconsecrated Theatre · Prague

Opening night, sold by post. No applause for the first twenty seconds after the final note. She took this as permission.
