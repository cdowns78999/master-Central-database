# Chart Platforms Research
> Every unique chart platform across 10 global markets — bracket architecture format
> Researched 2026-03-19 | 31 platforms across US, Japan, UK, Germany, China, France, South Korea, Australia, Brazil, Canada + global streaming

---

## Global Platforms (Multi-Market)

### Billboard
**Est. 1894** — Billboard was founded by William Donaldson and James Hennegan in Cincinnati, Ohio as a trade publication covering the bill posting and outdoor advertising industry. It pivoted toward entertainment in the early 1900s, covering circuses, fairs, and vaudeville, before publishing its first national music chart — the National List of Best Selling Retail Records — on July 27, 1940, and later launching the iconic Hot 100 in 1958, which combined sales, radio airplay, and (eventually) streaming into the single most cited chart in music history. It stands today as the undisputed global gold standard for chart credibility — a Billboard #1 remains the ultimate mainstream milestone, and the brand has expanded into Billboard Japan, Billboard Canada, and Billboard Global 200 charts spanning dozens of countries.

### Spotify Charts
**Est. 2006** — Spotify was founded in Stockholm, Sweden by Daniel Ek and Martin Lorentzon as a legal alternative to music piracy, after Ek watched Napster die and Kazaa thrive and decided the only solution was a service better than stealing. It officially launched on October 7, 2008 in Sweden before expanding to the US in 2011, growing from a European startup into the world's largest audio streaming platform through playlist-driven discovery and algorithmic recommendations that fundamentally changed how music gets consumed. It stands today with 675 million+ monthly active users and 260 million+ Premium subscribers, commanding roughly 31% global market share — Spotify Top 50 placements and editorial playlist adds drive career trajectories worldwide, making it the single most influential force in determining what music gets heard.

### Apple Music
**Est. 2015** — Apple Music was launched on June 30, 2015 as Apple's streaming successor to iTunes, catalyzed by Apple's $3 billion acquisition of Beats Electronics in 2014 which gave them Beats Music's streaming infrastructure and subscriber base. It rose to power by leveraging the largest installed device base on earth, bundling with iOS, curating editorial playlists with human tastemakers, and offering lossless Spatial Audio that audiophiles actually care about. It stands today as the #2 global streaming platform with approximately 100 million+ paying subscribers, carrying outsized influence in the US, Japan, and premium demographics where Apple device ownership skews high.

### Shazam
**Est. 2000** — Shazam was co-founded in London by Chris Barton, Philip Inghelbrecht, Dhiraj Mukherjee, and Avery Wang after Barton conceived the idea of phone-based song identification in 1999 during an MBA internship at Microsoft. It launched commercially in 2002 as a phone number you dialed (not an app), nearly went bankrupt for six years until the App Store launched in 2008 turned it into one of the first viral mobile apps, and was ultimately acquired by Apple in 2018 for $400 million. It stands today as the leading predictor of breakout hits with over 2 billion downloads — when someone Shazams a track, they're actively seeking it out, making the Shazam Top 200 an early-warning system that labels and A&Rs watch obsessively to spot what's about to blow up.

### YouTube Charts
**Est. 2005 (charts launched 2018)** — YouTube was founded as a video-sharing platform by Chad Hurley, Steve Chen, and Jawed Karim, acquired by Google for $1.65 billion in 2006, and became the world's de facto free music streaming service as billions of people watched music videos daily. YouTube formally launched its own music charts in May 2018 across 44 countries, introducing Top Songs, Top Artists, Top Music Videos, and a real-time Trending chart updated multiple times daily — formalizing what was already true: artists had been measuring success by view counts for a decade. It stands today as the most globally democratic chart system, reflecting actual audience behavior across every market with internet access, now integrated into YouTube Music's 100M+ subscriber ecosystem.

## United States

### SiriusXM BPM
**Est. 2001 (BPM channel launched 2011)** — SiriusXM was formed from the 2008 merger of Sirius Satellite Radio (founded 1990 as Satellite CD Radio) and XM Satellite Radio (founded 1992), creating North America's monopoly satellite radio provider with 150+ channels beamed to 34 million+ subscribers, primarily in cars. BPM (channel 51) launched as SiriusXM's dedicated EDM/dance channel, programmed by Geronimo Aala, featuring mainstream electronic acts and serving as the satellite radio gateway for dance music — its playlist and rotation data provides a unique signal of what's crossing over to mainstream American ears during commutes. It stands today as the only satellite radio EDM channel with national US reach, carrying particular weight in the car-listening demographic that streaming playlists don't fully capture — its "BPM Breakthrough" show and festival-season programming make it a meaningful promotional channel for electronic artists trying to reach beyond the streaming bubble.

## Japan

### Oricon
**Est. 1967** — Oricon (Original Confidence Inc.) was founded in Tokyo by former Snow Brand Milk Products promoter Soko Koike, publishing its first experimental singles chart in November 1967 before going official on January 4, 1968. Koike aggressively promoted the charts through television in the 1970s until the abbreviation "Oricon" became a household name by the late 1970s — becoming the definitive tracker of physical CD sales in a market where physical media never died. It stands today as Japan's most trusted chart authority, now incorporating streaming data alongside its legendary physical sales tracking, still managed by the founder's family after Koike's death and operating under the shortened name adopted in 1992.

### LINE MUSIC
**Est. 2015** — LINE MUSIC was launched on June 11, 2015 by LINE Corporation as an on-demand music streaming service integrated with Japan's dominant messaging app LINE, backed by deals with Sony Music Japan, Avex Group, Warner Music Group, Universal Music Group, and King Records. It rose to power through deep social integration — users can send tracks to friends, stream simultaneously during chats, and set songs as profile BGM — leveraging LINE's massive installed base across Japan. It stands today as one of Japan's top streaming platforms, particularly strong among younger Japanese demographics who live inside the LINE ecosystem and use music as a social expression tool within their chats.

### Recochoku
**Est. 2001** — Recochoku was founded in July 2001 as a joint venture by Japan's major domestic record labels (Sony Music, Avex, Universal Music, EMI, Victor, and others) to sell "Chaku Uta" ringtones — becoming the first Japanese website to sell ringtones and the country's largest music sales site during the feature phone (keitai) era. It dominated Japan's mobile music market through the 2000s before smartphones and iTunes eroded its position in the 2010s. It stands today as a legacy digital music and artist solutions provider, acquired by NexTone in September 2023 — its charts still serve as historical reference points for pre-streaming Japanese music popularity but no longer carry the weight they once did.

## United Kingdom

### UK Official Charts Company (OCC)
**Est. 1952 (formalized 1969)** — The UK's chart history began on November 14, 1952 when NME co-founder Percy Dickins compiled Britain's first Top 12 hit parade (Al Martino's "Here In My Heart" at #1), modeled on America's Billboard. The chart became "official" in February 1969 when the British Market Research Bureau (BMRB) began compiling it as a joint venture between the BBC and Record Retailer, evolving through Gallup (1983) and eventually forming the Official Charts Company as a joint venture equally owned by the BPI and the Entertainment Retailers Association. It stands today as the sole chart recognized by the British music industry, combining streaming, downloads, and physical sales — and has expanded internationally to compile official charts for Ireland (since 2012) and France (since 2021), making OCC a quiet power player far beyond British shores.

### BBC Radio 1
**Est. 1967** — BBC Radio 1 launched on September 30, 1967 as the BBC's answer to pirate radio stations like Radio Caroline that had been illegally broadcasting pop music to millions of British listeners from ships anchored in the North Sea. Tony Blackburn played the first record ("Flowers in the Rain" by The Move), and the station quickly became Britain's national pop and rock radio institution, evolving through the decades from Top 40 pop into a tastemaker powerhouse for new music and electronic genres. It stands today as a critical kingmaker for dance and electronic music — Annie Mac (now Annie Macmanus, who left in 2021), Pete Tong's "Essential Mix" (running since 1993), and Danny Howard's dance shows have launched countless EDM careers, and a Radio 1 playlist add remains one of the most coveted promotional wins in British music, reaching 9 million+ weekly listeners.

## Germany

### GfK Entertainment / Offizielle Deutsche Charts
**Est. 1977** — Germany's official charts first appeared in September 1977 in the trade magazine Der Musikmarkt, compiled by research institute Media Control on behalf of the Bundesverband Musikindustrie (German Music Industry Association), after years of testing different providers since charts were first informally published in West Germany in 1959. Media Control merged with market research giant GfK in 2003 (rebranded to GfK Entertainment in 2014), building Europe's most methodologically rigorous chart system that tracks point-of-sale data from 3,000+ German retail outlets transmitted weekly via the PhonoNet communication network. It stands today as the sole official German chart provider — now under the Circana umbrella — covering singles, albums, streaming, and genre-specific rankings including dance/electronic, with 100 chart positions (expanded from the original 50 in 1989).

### FEIYR / Dance-Charts.de
**Est. 2004** — Dance-Charts.de was launched in 2004 as an independent German-language platform tracking DJ reactions and dance music chart performance, eventually partnering with FEIYR, a digital music distribution and aggregation company founded in Stuttgart. Unlike the official GfK charts which measure consumer sales and streams, Dance-Charts.de compiles its rankings from DJ feedback pools — DJs rate and report which tracks they're actually playing in clubs and on radio, creating a grassroots industry chart that reflects the dance floor rather than the download store. It stands today as Germany's most relevant specialist dance music chart, widely referenced by German DJs, dance labels, and promoters as a complementary signal to the official charts — its DJ Top 50 and Genre charts (Techno, House, Trance, etc.) provide granular electronic music data that the mainstream Offizielle Deutsche Charts simply don't capture at that level of detail.

## China

### QQ Music / Tencent Music (TME)
**Est. 2005** — QQ Music was launched by Tencent in 2005 as a natural extension of the QQ messaging ecosystem that already had 800 million+ users across China. It leveraged Tencent's massive user base and aggressive exclusive licensing deals with major labels to crush domestic competitors, then merged with China Music Corporation (owners of Kugou and Kuwo) in 2016 to form Tencent Music Entertainment Group (TME), which IPO'd on the NYSE in December 2018, raising approximately $1.1 billion. It stands today as China's #1 music streaming service — TME's combined platforms command roughly 75% of mainland China's music market, and QQ Music's charts are the closest thing China has to a Billboard: the definitive measure of mainstream popularity.

### NetEase Cloud Music
**Est. 2013** — NetEase Cloud Music was launched on April 23, 2013 by Chinese internet giant NetEase (founded by William Ding) as a community-driven alternative to QQ Music's mainstream-focused catalog approach. It rose to power through its social features — comment sections under songs became cultural phenomena, with some tracks accumulating millions of user comments, creating an emotional layer no competitor could replicate — hitting 100 million users in just two years. It stands today as China's #2 streaming platform with 200M+ monthly active users, known for championing indie, electronic, and alternative artists that QQ Music's mainstream focus overlooks.

### Kugou Music
**Est. 2004** — Kugou Music was created by Xie Zhenyu, who evolved his earlier Sougua Network platform into Kugou after facing competition from Baidu MP3, launching as a peer-to-peer music sharing and streaming service that became a grassroots favorite across China. It was absorbed into Tencent Music Entertainment when China Music Corporation merged with QQ Music in 2016, but has maintained its distinct identity and user base. It stands today as one of China's top 3 music apps with 800 million+ registered users, particularly dominant in lower-tier cities and among older demographics — its charts reflect grassroots popularity and karaoke culture rather than label-driven promotion.

### Kuwo Music
**Est. 2005** — Kuwo Music was founded in 2005 in Beijing as a streaming and download platform that carved out a distinct niche by focusing heavily on in-car listening and hardware integration — partnering with Chinese automakers to embed Kuwo as the default music app in vehicle infotainment systems across dozens of domestic car brands. Like Kugou, it was absorbed into Tencent Music Entertainment through the 2016 CMC-QQ Music merger, operating as TME's third pillar alongside QQ Music and Kugou. It stands today as a significant player in China's music ecosystem with 200M+ registered users, carrying particular weight in the driving demographic and tier-2/tier-3 cities — its charts reflect a more casual, ambient listening pattern distinct from the lean-forward engagement of QQ Music or NetEase.

### Douyin (TikTok CN)
**Est. 2016** — Douyin was launched in September 2016 by ByteDance (founded by Zhang Yiming) as a short-form video platform designed specifically for the Chinese market, originally called A.me before rebranding to Douyin ("shaking sound"). It amassed 100 million users within its first year and became the most powerful music discovery engine in China — labels now allocate the vast majority of their marketing budgets to Douyin, and the platform's "hot tracks" command massive market share of new music consumption. It stands today as the single most influential force in Chinese music virality — even QQ Music's own "TikTok Chart" ranks first among all their featured charts, proving that Douyin doesn't just host trends, it creates the entire ecosystem that other platforms chase.

## France

### SNEP (Syndicat National de l'Edition Phonographique)
**Est. 1922** — SNEP was founded on May 5, 1922 by Albert Bernard in Paris as the "Chambre syndicale de l'industrie et du commerce français des machines parlantes" (trade union for the French talking-machine industry), making it one of the oldest music industry bodies in the world. It became the official French chart authority, with the modern chart era beginning on November 4, 1984 when the Top 50 television chart show debuted on Canal+, turning the weekly French chart into appointment viewing. It stands today as the sole official French chart body, though in a notable shift, the London-based Official Charts Company (OCC) took over chart compilation from GfK on January 1, 2021 — a SNEP #1 remains the French equivalent of a Billboard #1, combining streaming, downloads, and physical sales with silver, gold, platinum, and diamond certifications.

### Deezer
**Est. 2007** — Deezer was founded on August 22, 2007 in Paris by 22-year-old Daniel Marhely and Jonathan Benassaya after Marhely was forced to shut down his music site blogmusik.net for copyright infringement and rebuilt it legally by securing an agreement with SACEM (France's copyright management society). It attracted 773,000 visitors in its first month and rose by securing early telecom partnerships across France, Brazil, and Africa — markets Spotify was slow to enter. It stands today as a global streaming platform with approximately 9-10 million paying subscribers, roughly 3-4 million of them in France — ranking behind the major global players but carrying outsized influence in French-speaking markets, Brazil, and emerging economies where its regional charts capture audiences the US-centric platforms miss.

## South Korea

### Melon
**Est. 2004** — Melon ("Melody On") was founded on November 16, 2004 by SK Telecom, South Korea's largest wireless carrier, as a digital music product promoted alongside MP3-capable phones in response to Korea's revised Copyright Act. It quickly became the country's dominant streaming platform during K-pop's explosive growth era, changing hands from SK Telecom to Loen Entertainment (2008) to Kakao (2016 acquisition for $1.64 billion) before merging with Kakao Entertainment in 2021. It stands today as South Korea's largest music subscription service with 28 million+ users and roughly 70% domestic market share — a Melon chart-topping remains the ultimate achievement in Korean pop music, and fan mobilization around Melon's real-time rankings is a defining feature of K-pop culture worldwide.

### Genie Music
**Est. 1991 (streaming launched 2011)** — Genie Music traces its roots to Blue Cord Technology's music contents division, established in 1991, eventually becoming a subsidiary of KT Corporation (Korea's largest telecom). It underwent major restructuring in 2009 as KT Music, acquired KMP Holdings in 2012 (a distributor owned by SM Entertainment and YG Entertainment), and rebranded as Genie Music after LG U+ invested as the second-largest shareholder. It stands today as Korea's #2 streaming platform with real-time charts that serve as a critical cross-reference to Melon — labels and fans track both platforms to gauge true market performance beyond single-platform manipulation campaigns.

### Circle Chart (formerly Gaon)
**Est. 2010** — Circle Chart was launched on February 23, 2010 as "Gaon Chart" by the Korea Music Content Association (KMCA), sponsored by the South Korean Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism, with the explicit aim of creating a national chart equivalent to Billboard (US) or Oricon (Japan) to replace the fragmented private rankings that broadcasters and media had been relying on. It was rebranded from Gaon to Circle Chart on July 7, 2022 to signal a global K-pop focus, incorporating data from Spotify, Apple Music, Melon, and Genie to reflect worldwide consumption trends alongside domestic data. It stands today as South Korea's sole official national music chart — the government-backed, industry-standard benchmark that provides the objective measurement K-pop's massive ecosystem needs to operate at global scale.

### Bugs Music
**Est. 1999** — Bugs was established in November 1999 and launched as a subscription digital music service in February 2000, making it one of the earliest streaming services in South Korea — by 2002 it had become the country's largest streaming service with over 9 million registered users. It operated under NHN Entertainment Corporation for years, carving a niche as the audiophile's choice with millions of tracks available in lossless FLAC format. It stands today as a smaller but respected South Korean streaming platform with a catalog of 40 million+ tracks — its hi-res audio focus gives it a loyal niche audience even as Melon and Genie dominate the mainstream charts, and its download-weighted chart methodology provides a different angle on Korean music consumption.

## Australia

### ARIA (Australian Recording Industry Association)
**Est. 1983** — ARIA was founded in 1983 by six major record companies (EMI, Festival, CBS, RCA, WEA, and Universal) as a not-for-profit trade body replacing the Association of Australian Record Manufacturers (AARM, formed 1956), with the first ARIA-branded national Top 50 chart published the week ending July 10, 1983. It launched the inaugural ARIA Music Awards in 1987 at Sydney's Sheraton Wentworth Hotel and transitioned to fully in-house chart compilation by mid-1988, building out singles, albums, and genre-specific charts including a dedicated Dance chart. It stands today as Australia's only official chart body — the ARIA Charts and annual ARIA Awards are the definitive benchmarks for commercial success in the Australian music industry, combining streaming, downloads, and physical sales.

### triple j
**Est. 1975** — triple j launched on January 19, 1975 as "2JJ" from offices in Darlinghurst, Sydney, as the Australian Broadcasting Corporation's national youth radio station — the first legal FM rock station in Australia, created to serve young listeners who felt alienated by mainstream commercial radio. It became a cultural institution through its "Unearthed" unsigned artist discovery program (launched 1995, now a standalone digital platform) and the annual Hottest 100 countdown — the world's largest music poll, with over 3 million votes cast each year, held on the fourth weekend of January. It stands today as Australia's most influential tastemaker for alternative, electronic, and indie music, broadcasting to 2 million+ weekly listeners across every major Australian city — a triple j add or Hottest 100 placement has launched the careers of Flume, Tame Impala, Rufus Du Sol, and countless other acts who went from Australian unknowns to global headliners.

## Brazil

### Palco MP3
**Est. 2003** — Palco MP3 was founded in 2003 in Recife, northeastern Brazil, by Pablo Lins and Vinicius Baggio as a free platform where independent Brazilian musicians could upload and distribute their own music directly to fans — essentially a Brazilian Bandcamp/SoundCloud hybrid years before either existed. It exploded in popularity across Brazil's massive independent music scene, particularly among sertanejo, forró, funk carioca, and regional genres that major labels and international platforms ignored entirely. It stands today as Brazil's largest independent music platform with 6 million+ registered artists and 100 million+ monthly plays, serving as the primary digital home for Brazil's vibrant regional music ecosystem — its charts reflect what everyday Brazilians are actually listening to at the grassroots level, making it an essential signal for anyone tracking music trends in Latin America's largest market.

### Vagalume
**Est. 2002** — Vagalume (Portuguese for "firefly") was founded in 2002 as a Brazilian lyrics website, built around the simple insight that Portuguese-speaking music fans desperately needed accurate song lyrics in their own language — a need that global platforms like Genius wouldn't address for over a decade. It grew into a full music discovery platform with artist pages, music news, user-generated content, and listening data that captures Brazilian consumption patterns across all genres. It stands today as one of Brazil's most-visited music websites with tens of millions of monthly visitors, functioning as a cultural barometer — its trending and most-searched data provides real-time insight into what songs are catching fire in Brazil, often ahead of streaming chart movements, making it a useful leading indicator for the Brazilian market.

### Sua Musica
**Est. 2008** — Sua Musica (Portuguese for "Your Music") was founded in Recife, in Brazil's northeast, as a platform specifically built for forró, sertanejo, piseiro, brega funk, and other regional genres that dominate Brazilian culture outside the Rio-São Paulo axis but were completely invisible on international platforms. It grew rapidly by serving the massive audience that lives and breathes regional Brazilian music — genres that fill stadiums and generate billions of streams domestically but barely register on Spotify's global radar. It stands today as the leading platform for northeastern Brazilian music with 15 million+ monthly active users, hosting over 400,000 artists — its charts are the definitive measure of grassroots popularity in Brazil's regional music scene, capturing a market segment that no global streaming service adequately tracks.

## Canada

### CBC Music
**Est. 1936 (as CBC Radio)** — The Canadian Broadcasting Corporation was established on November 2, 1936 as Canada's national public broadcaster, with its radio service becoming a cornerstone of Canadian cultural identity — CBC Radio has broadcast continuously for nearly 90 years, making it one of the oldest national broadcasters in the Americas. CBC Music evolved from the Radio 2/Radio 3 divisions, with Radio 3 (launched 2000 as an online-only service) specifically championing emerging Canadian artists and electronic music through podcasts, playlists, and the CBC Music website. It stands today as Canada's primary public platform for music discovery and Canadian artist support, with particular relevance to the electronic and indie scenes — its curated playlists, live session recordings, and the annual Searchlight competition serve as a nationally trusted signal for emerging Canadian talent, and CBC Radio One/Music reaches 7 million+ weekly listeners across the country.

## Dance/Electronic Specialist

### Traxsource
**Est. 2004** — Traxsource was launched in October 2004 by DJs and producers Brian Tappert and Marc Pomeroy, who had been planning since 2000 after watching pirate sites fill the digital demand for their Soulfuric Records catalog while their distributor went bankrupt after 9/11. They built it as a curated digital music store specifically for house music DJs — Deep, Soulful, Tech, Afro — at a time when mainstream digital stores ignored the underground entirely. It stands today as the premier marketplace for house and dance music, featuring 290+ DJs contributing to its Most Wanted charts, downloads in MP3/WAV/AIFF formats, and a Hype Chart that serves as the underground's equivalent of a Billboard placement — if you're a house music producer and you chart on Traxsource, the right people are paying attention.
