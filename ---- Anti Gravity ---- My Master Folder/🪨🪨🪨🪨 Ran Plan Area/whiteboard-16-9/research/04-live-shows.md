# Live Shows — Research Findings

## Key Findings

- **Independent venues are the backbone of the live ecosystem but are structurally unprofitable.** NIVA's 2025 *State of Live* report (prepared by TEConomy Partners) found that 64% of independent venues nationwide were unprofitable in 2024, even as the indie-live sector contributed over $86B to US GDP and powers a $153B broader economy. State-level unprofitability is even higher: 69% in California, 80% in Ohio, 81% in New York ([NIVA State of Live 2025](https://www.nivassoc.org/news/2025/nivas-state-of-live-report-shows-independent-venues-are-essential-but-under-threat)).

- **Pollstar's 2025 year-end data shows a "hollowing-out" of the live market.** Stadiums (30,000+ cap) posted record grosses while clubs and theaters contracted, with NIVA, NITO, and IEBA jointly reporting that the club sector faces "greater difficulty selling tickets, lower bar sales, rising expenses, shaky consumer confidence" — demand for 2026 club/theater shows is projected flat-to-down as consumers choose stadium tours over local acts ([Pollstar Year-End 2025](https://news.pollstar.com/2025/12/23/year-end-business-analysis-a-return-to-earth-2025-grosses-ticket-sales-drop-averages-increase-beyonce-oasis-coldplay-have-top-tours-venues-stadiums-rock/)).

- **Ticketmaster consent decree extended 8 years; $280M settlement fund; 15% service-fee cap on amphitheaters.** In March 2026 Live Nation settled with DOJ to cap ticketing fees at 15% on amphitheaters, let promoters distribute up to 50% of tickets, open amphitheaters to all promoters, and divest 13 exclusive booking arrangements. However, 30+ state AGs rejected the settlement as insufficient, and in April 2026 a federal jury separately found Live Nation/Ticketmaster to be an illegal monopoly, determining Ticketmaster overcharged fans by $1.72 per ticket in the plaintiff jurisdictions ([Music Business Worldwide — Live Nation antitrust verdict](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/live-nation-antitrust-verdict-what-happened-what-it-means-and-what-comes-next/)).

- **Mid-tier festivals are collapsing — 100+ festivals canceled worldwide in 2025.** More than 40 US/UK festivals were canceled before summer 2025 even started, and GA prices on majors have climbed 55% since 2014. The mid-tier model — propped up by corporate brand activations — fractured when sponsors slashed experiential budgets. Coachella 2024 took 27 days to sell out Weekend 1 (vs. hours historically) and 2025 again failed to sell out. Bonnaroo 2025 was weather-canceled after one night; Pitchfork Music Festival ended its run; Desert Daze went dark ([CNN — decline of music festivals](https://www.cnn.com/2025/06/01/entertainment/music-festivals-cancellations-pitchfork-cec); [Billboard — Coachella sluggish](https://www.billboard.com/business/touring/coachella-ticket-sales-lower-than-usual-2024-festival-trend-1235655410/)).

- **Merch at the venue remains the single highest-margin revenue an artist can touch.** atVenu's 2024–2025 data shows the industry-average merch $/head rose +14% in 2024, K-pop +13% again in 2025, and the average touring artist grossed $10K per show in merch sales. Artists typically keep 75–80% of net merch profit on tour deals. For comparative scale: one T-shirt sold ≈ 8,750 streams in net revenue, and 61% of Spotify artists earned less in a full year of royalties than a single show grosses in merch. A standing industry fight: some venues still try to skim 10–25% off artist merch, a practice NIVA-aligned indies have largely abandoned but arenas frequently impose ([atVenu — Merch vs Streaming](https://www.atvenu.com/post/how-much-money-artists-make-in-streaming-vs-merchandise-sales); [atVenu 2025 Mid-Year](https://www.atvenu.com/post/mid-year-venue-merchandise-insights-how-fan-spending-is-evolving)).

- **Door-split and guarantee structures are well-defined at the club level.** Standard club deals are 70/30 or 80/20 of NET door (after agreed expenses) to the artist, with "versus deals" (greater of guarantee-or-percentage) now the dominant hybrid for developing acts. Pure guarantees are reserved for established headliners; openers and emerging acts almost always take door splits or a flat bar-minimum. In 2026, door deals remain the default for any situation where both sides are gambling on turnout ([DIY Musician CD Baby — Guarantees vs Door Splits](https://diymusician.cdbaby.com/music-career/band-booking-guarantees-vs-door-splits/); [Ticket Fairy — Booking Contracts 2026](https://www.ticketfairy.com/blog/deal-or-no-deal-navigating-booking-contracts-settlements-for-venues-in-2026)).

- **Insurance is now a top-three existential cost for small venues post-Astroworld.** General liability premiums for live-music rooms spiked sharply after the 2021 Astroworld disaster; in Austin a single mid-size music venue with a $2M liability requirement reported paying $80K/year in premiums. NIVA's 2025 report flagged insurance alongside rent, artist fees, and staffing as the top cost-growth categories, with 60% of venues expecting artist fees to rise and 58% expecting staffing costs to climb in 2025 ([Austin Monitor — Insurance Costs at City Summit](https://austinmonitor.com/stories/2023/08/insurance-costs-listed-among-music-venues-biggest-challenges-at-city-summit/); [NIVA State of Live 2025](https://www.nivassoc.org/news/2025/nivas-state-of-live-report-shows-independent-venues-are-essential-but-under-threat)).

- **Ticketing alternatives (DICE, See Tickets, Eventbrite) are consolidating around the indie circuit.** DICE works only with independent venues and promoters in the 200–10,000 capacity range, with 90% of fans saying it's easier to use than Ticketmaster and 85% saying it's more transparent. In June 2025 Fever acquired DICE to form the largest independent live-tech platform globally; DICE continues as a standalone app. In the UK, a voluntary £1 LIVE Trust levy on 5,000+ cap shows (via Tixel checkout and artist opt-in — Pulp, Mumford & Sons, Diana Ross, Hans Zimmer) raised £500K+ in pledges in its first months as a grassroots-venue rescue mechanism ([Music Ally — Fever acquires DICE](https://musically.com/2025/06/05/fever-acquires-ticketing-firm-dice-in-live-tech-consolidation/); [IQ Magazine — LIVE Trust levy](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2025/05/tixel-to-introduce-levy-for-live-trust/)).

## Why It Matters for Artists

For an independent artist in 2026, live shows are simultaneously the most viable revenue floor (merch at the venue beats streaming by orders of magnitude) and the most economically fragile surface — the 200–2,000 cap room where most developing acts play is the exact tier that is unprofitable at 64% of venues and contracting year-over-year. The consolidation of ticketing power (even post-DOJ settlement), the collapse of mid-tier festivals that used to function as exposure engines, and skyrocketing insurance + artist-fee costs mean the economics of "play more shows" no longer scales linearly. The artist-actionable reality: lean into direct-to-fan ticketing (DICE, venue box office), maximize merch per head at every show, and treat the club/theater circuit as a margin-thin retail moment where the margin comes from what you sell on the table — not from the guarantee.

## Artist-Actionable Implications

- **Treat merch as the primary revenue line on a club tour, not an afterthought.** Industry average is now $10K gross per show and growing +14% YoY; build the setlist/performance flow around a merch call-out, staff the table, and negotiate 0% venue merch-cut into your contract rider (standard at NIVA-member indie rooms, a bargaining chip at larger venues).
- **Default to "versus" deals (greater of guarantee or % of door) at the 200–1,000 cap level** rather than pure door-splits that leave all risk on the artist, or guarantees that cap upside on a hot night. Ask for 80/20 net door as the percentage side whenever possible.
- **Use DICE, See Tickets, or Eventbrite for direct-to-fan tickets** — fee transparency, better data ownership, and no Ticketmaster lock-in. In a 200–2,000 cap venue world, Ticketmaster is no longer the distribution requirement it is at arena level.
- **Skip the mid-tier festival bet; prioritize destination clubs + city-anchored runs.** With 100+ festivals canceled in 2025 and sponsors retreating, festival bookings are a shakier exposure vehicle in 2026 than a well-promoted 4-to-6-city regional club run with local openers and press.
- **Budget for weather/force-majeure cancellation and ticket-holder refunds** in artist side-deals. Bonnaroo 2025 and a string of UK festivals cratered on single-event weather calls; the artist contract should specify cancellation-fee treatment and whether the guarantee is paid regardless.
