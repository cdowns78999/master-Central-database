What an artist walks away with

core takeaways
  • Three deal shapesGuarantee (fixed, promoter risk), door deal (70–85% of door, shared risk), and greater-of (floor + upside) — every show offer is one of these three.
  • $100 ticket = $8–$15 netAfter venue, ticketing, production, crew and band, a $100 ticket nets the artist roughly $8–$15. Plan accordingly.
  • Merch is the lifeline~$15/fan in merch is often the difference between break-even and going home in debt; t-shirts at $30–$45 signal value.
  • Cost stack is brutalVan $100–$300/day, gas $200–$600/show, hotel $100–$250/night, food $50–$100/person/day stack fast — a 10-show regional tour can run $8K–$15K easily.
  • Route as a loop, not a zigzagGeographic logic beats opportunity-order routing — avoid the 800-mile penalty Orphiq calls out by sequencing shows in a circular path.
  • Build local before going nationalTouring before a local fanbase exists generates no draw, empty rooms, and venues that refuse to rebook — local first, every time.
  • Email list at every showThe in-person fan converts to streams, pre-saves, and future ticket buyers at rates far above cold digital advertising.
  • Treat early tours as investmentExpecting profit from a first national tour leads to financial stress — budget early tours as audience-building investment, not revenue.

Deal Shapes

Guarantee · Door · Greater-Of

Reference · Key Concept

Three structures, one offer sheet

Every live show offer reduces to one of three structures. Knowing which one you're being offered — and which one you should be asking for — is the first negotiation skill of a touring artist.

  • Guarantee: "the artist receives a fixed payment regardless of ticket sales… typical club-level guarantees range from $200–$5,000 per show for independents." (Band Vans)
  • Door deal: "70–85% of the door after the venue takes its cut. The artist shares financial risk with the promoter." (gigmit)
  • Greater-of: "A guaranteed floor plus the right to receive their percentage if ticket sales exceed what the guarantee would cover." (Arts Hacker)

Merch Lifeline

$15/fan · Often = profit

Reference · Key Concept

Why the merch table matters more than the door

Merch is one of the few line items on a tour where the artist captures full margin. It's also the fastest path from break-even to profitable.

  • "Merchandise sales at the show average $15 per attending fan and are often the difference between breaking even and going home in debt."
  • "Fans who want to support their favorite artist will pay $30–$45 for a quality t-shirt. Underpriced merch signals low value and leaves significant revenue uncaptured." (Orphiq)
  • "Every in-person fan is the most valuable fan in the ecosystem. An email list built at shows converts to streaming listeners, pre-save participants, and future ticket buyers at rates far above cold digital advertising."

Cost Truth

$100 ticket = $8–$15 net

Reference · Key Concept

What a $100 ticket actually pays

"Artist net from a $100 ticket: approximately $8–$15 after paying venue, ticketing fees, production costs, crew, and band members." Tour cost stacks compound this further:

  • Van rental $100–$300/day
  • Gas $200–$600/show depending on routing distance
  • Lodging $100–$250/night
  • Food $50–$100/person/day
  • Equipment rental (if needed) $200–$800/show
  • "A 10-show regional tour" can easily cost $8K–$15K before merch.
