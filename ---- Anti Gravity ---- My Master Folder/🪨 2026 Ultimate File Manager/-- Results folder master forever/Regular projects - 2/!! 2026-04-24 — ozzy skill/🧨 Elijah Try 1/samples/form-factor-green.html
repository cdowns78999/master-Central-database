Record Labels — Major vs Indie

Record labels fund recording, manufacturing, marketing, and distribution in exchange for a share of an artist's revenue and often ownership of the master recording.

Key Facts 4 of 6
01

Traditional Recording Deal

Label owns the master recording, advances recording costs against future royalties, and pays the artist 10–20% of revenue. The artist must recoup the advance before seeing any royalty income.

02

360 Deal — Multiple Rights

Label takes 15–25% of all artist revenue streams — touring, merch, brand deals, publishing — in addition to recording royalties. Became standard at majors in the 2010s as album revenue declined.

03

Licensing Deal

Artist retains master ownership and licenses the recording to the label for a fixed term (5–7 years). At term end, masters revert to the artist — common for established indies with leverage.

04

Joint Venture (JV)

Label funds all recording and marketing, keeps a running ledger of expenses, and after recoupment splits profits 50/50 with the artist. More equitable, but requires real commercial belief.

Takeaways Common Mistakes
  • Avoid: Signing a 360 deal that captures touring and merch — those are the only guaranteed revenue streams for most artists, and giving away 15–25% of them is often financially devastating.
  • Avoid: Misreading recoupment — labels recover advances, recording costs, and sometimes marketing costs before the artist sees a royalty dollar. An artist can go platinum and still be unrecouped on paper.
  • Avoid: Conflating advances with salary — a label advance is a loan against future royalties. Every dollar must be earned back before royalties are paid, which can take years.
