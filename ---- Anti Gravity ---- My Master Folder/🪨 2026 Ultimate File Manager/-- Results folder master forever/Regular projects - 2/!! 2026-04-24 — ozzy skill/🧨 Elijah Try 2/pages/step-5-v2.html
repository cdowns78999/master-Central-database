Master vs. Publishing

TWO PARALLEL PIPELINES

Master rights are owned by whoever financed the sound recording — label or independent artist. Publishing rights belong to the songwriter(s) and their publisher. Two separate pipelines, two separate paychecks.

5 Royalty Types

PERFORMANCE · MECHANICAL · SYNC · MASTER · NEIGHBORING

Performance, mechanical, sync, master recording, and neighboring rights — each generated by a different use, and each collected by a different body. Knowing which is which is the difference between getting paid and leaving money on the table.

Collection Bodies

PRO · MLC · SOUNDEXCHANGE · ADMIN

PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC) collect performance royalties. MLC collects mechanicals from US DSPs. SoundExchange collects digital performance for masters. Publishing admins register, collect, and chase the rest worldwide.

05

What an artist walks away with

core takeaways
  • Two pipelines, alwaysEvery song generates master royalties (the recording) and publishing royalties (the composition). They flow through entirely separate plumbing — never assume one covers the other.
  • 50 / 50 writer-publisherEvery publishing royalty splits 50/50 between writer's share and publisher's share by default in most territories. If you have no publisher, you're leaving the publisher's half on the table.
  • Register with a PRO before releasePerformance royalties accumulate from the moment a song goes live — but they cannot be paid to an unregistered writer. Register with ASCAP or BMI before you ship.
  • The MLC is not optionalIndependent artists who haven't registered with the MLC or a publishing administrator are leaving 15–25% of their total streaming income uncollected.
  • Sync = two clearancesFor TV/film/ads, both the master (sound recording) and publishing (composition) sides must be cleared separately — two negotiations, two checks.
  • Split sheet on day oneFile a split sheet the moment a song is co-written. Informal handshake agreements about splits are extremely difficult to enforce once the song generates money.
  • Indie keeps more master, signed gets servicesAn independent artist who owns their masters keeps the full ~$0.58 master share; a signed artist trades a chunk of that for advances, marketing, and label muscle.
  • Admin ≠ full publisherA publishing admin collects and registers; a true publisher also pitches songs for sync and co-writes. Different deal structures, very different commissions.

M

Master vs. Publishing

TWO PARALLEL PIPELINES

Reference · Key Concept

The two-pipeline model

"Master rights: owned by whoever financed or owns the sound recording — typically the label in a signed deal, or the artist themselves if independent. Master royalties flow from DSPs to label/distributor to artist." — Monetunes

  • Master side — DSP pays the rights holder (label or distributor), who then pays the artist per their deal.
  • Publishing side — DSPs separately pay mechanicals to MLC and performance fees to PROs; the writer's share goes direct to the songwriter.
  • Independent artists who own their masters receive the full master royalty through their distributor.
  • Songwriters must be registered with both a PRO and the MLC (or a publishing admin) to collect every dollar owed.
5

5 Royalty Types

THE FULL STACK

Reference · Key Concept

Every royalty type, mapped

"Performance royalties are collected by PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC in the US; PRS in the UK; SOCAN in Canada) whenever music is broadcast publicly — radio, TV, streaming, restaurants, venues." — Ari's Take

  • Performance — public broadcast (radio, TV, streaming, venues). Collected by PROs.
  • Mechanical — every reproduction (CDs, vinyl, on-demand streams). Collected by the MLC in the US.
  • Sync — one-time negotiated fee when a song is licensed for visual media. Both master and publishing sides cleared separately.
  • Master recording — paid by DSPs to the recording owner (label or independent artist).
  • Neighboring / digital performance — for the master side on non-interactive radio. Collected by SoundExchange.
C

Collection Bodies

WHO COLLECTS WHAT

Reference · Key Concept

The four collection bodies

"Mechanical royalties are generated every time a song is reproduced — physical sales (CDs, vinyl) and on-demand digital streams. In the US, the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC) collects mechanical royalties from DSPs and distributes to publishers." — Step 5 source notes

  • PRO (ASCAP / BMI / SESAC) — performance royalties for the writer + publisher.
  • MLC — US digital mechanicals, paid to publishers / self-administered writers.
  • SoundExchange — digital performance for the master side (SiriusXM, Pandora non-interactive, webcasting).
  • Publishing admin (Songtrust, Sentric) — registers compositions globally and chases everything else.
