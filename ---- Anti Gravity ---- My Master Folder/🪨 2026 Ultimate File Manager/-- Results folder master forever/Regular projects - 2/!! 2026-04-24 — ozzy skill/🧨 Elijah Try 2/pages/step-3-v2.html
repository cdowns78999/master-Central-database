What an artist walks away with

core takeaways from Step 3
  • Match the model to your release cadenceHigh-volume releasers save money on flat-fee distributors (DistroKid $24.99/yr, TuneCore $14.99–$49.99/yr); infrequent releasers can use CD Baby's per-project model.
  • Always register an ISRCWithout ISRCs, streaming plays may not be accurately tracked, resulting in missed or mis-attributed royalties.
  • Bundle publishing admin if you need itTuneCore and CD Baby both offer integrated publishing admin — without it, mechanical royalties can go uncollected from streams.
  • UnitedMasters for brand dealsFree tier takes 10% of earnings; Select tier costs $5/month and keeps 100% of royalties. Brand-deal marketplace is the differentiator.
  • Amuse for zero-budget entryFree distribution tier with unlimited releases is the lowest-friction starting point for first-time artists.
  • Set up royalty splits up frontUsing one distributor account for personal and collaborative releases without proper splits creates payout disputes and accounting headaches.
  • Plan around the 1–14 day delivery windowUpload → validate → API delivery → DSP ingest → live takes time. Schedule release dates with buffer.
  • Avoid revenue-share trap at scalePaying 15–30% of all streaming income indefinitely costs far more than a $25/year flat-fee subscription once volume picks up.

Reference · Comparison Frame

Five distributors, four cost structures

The five dominant independent distributors in 2025 split into four cost models: flat annual fee (DistroKid, TuneCore), per-release one-time fee (CD Baby), free-with-revenue-share (UnitedMasters free, Amuse free), and paid-subscription-no-cut (UnitedMasters Select, Amuse Pro).

  • DistroKid: $24.99/yr, 0% cut, royalty splits built-in.
  • TuneCore: $14.99–$49.99/yr tiered, 0% cut, optional publishing admin.
  • CD Baby: $9.99 single / $29 album, 9% cut, integrated publishing admin.
  • UnitedMasters: Free (10% cut) or $5/mo (0% cut), brand-deal marketplace.
  • Amuse: Free tier, unlimited releases, 0% cut on free tier.

Reference · IP Layer

Why ISRCs are non-negotiable

The ISRC (International Standard Recording Code) is a 12-character identifier assigned to a specific recording. It travels with the track across every DSP, scrobbler, and royalty collection society — it's how plays are matched back to a rights holder.

  • "Without ISRCs, streaming plays may not be accurately tracked, resulting in missed or mis-attributed royalties."
  • Independent artists should register ISRCs for every track.
  • Most distributors auto-issue an ISRC at upload — but verify it gets attached before the release goes live.
  • An ISRC is per-recording, not per-song. A re-recording or remix needs its own ISRC.

Reference · Operational Flow

From upload to live on Spotify

The DSP delivery pipeline begins when an artist submits their track through a distributor's portal. The distributor checks audio quality, validates metadata (ISRC code, UPC barcode, artist name, songwriter credits), and then ingests the content into each DSP's system via direct API connections.

  • Audio: WAV or FLAC, 16-bit or 24-bit.
  • Artwork: 3000x3000px JPG.
  • Metadata: ISRC + UPC + artist name + songwriter credits + release date.
  • Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon Music, Tidal, Deezer, YouTube Music — each ingests separately.
  • Total time from upload to live: typically 1–14 days, depending on the distributor and DSP.
