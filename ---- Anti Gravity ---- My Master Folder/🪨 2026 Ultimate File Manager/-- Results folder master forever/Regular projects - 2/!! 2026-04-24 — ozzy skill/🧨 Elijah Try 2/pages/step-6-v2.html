Key Concepts — The Three Slices of a Song

Master vs Composition

Two Different Copyrights

Every song has TWO copyrights. The master = the recording (owned by the artist or label). The composition = the melody + lyrics (owned by the songwriter and/or their publisher). Publishing royalties only ever come from the composition side.

Open spec ↗

Writer's Share Forever Yours

50/50 Split — Always

Composition royalties always split 50/50 between the writer's share and the publisher's share. The writer's share goes DIRECTLY to the songwriter via their PRO. No publisher can ever take it. The publisher's share is the only piece negotiable in a deal.

Open spec ↗

3 Deal Types

Admin · Co-Pub · Full Pub

Admin deals: keep 100% copyright, pay 10–25% commission. Co-pub: hand over 50% of copyright for life. Full pub: hand over 100% for the life of the copyright (70+ years past your death). Pick wrong and your kids inherit nothing.

What an artist walks away with

core takeaways
  • Two copyrights, alwaysThe master (recording) and the composition (melody + lyrics) are legally distinct. Negotiate them separately or you'll give one away by accident.
  • Writer's share is sacredComposition royalties always split 50/50 writer/publisher. The writer's share is yours forever and flows directly through your PRO — no publisher can take it.
  • Admin ≠ PublisherAn admin collects royalties for a 10–25% cut and gives the copyright back when the deal ends. A publisher co-owns the copyright. These are fundamentally different agreements.
  • Co-pub is the standard mid-tier dealCo-publishing assigns 50% of your copyright to the publisher — often for the life of the copyright (70+ years after death). Read the reversion clause carefully.
  • Full pub is increasingly rarePer Songtrust and ASCAP, full publishing deals are "increasingly rare and generally unfavorable" for artists who can self-administer.
  • 3 steps to indie publishing(1) Register with a PRO (ASCAP / BMI). (2) Sign with a publishing administrator (Songtrust / TuneCore Publishing). (3) Register every release with PRO + admin + MLC.
  • Don't skip PRO registrationPerformance royalties accumulate from day one but are only payable to registered writers. Unclaimed cash gets reabsorbed into the rights-holder pool.
  • Co-writers must each registerIf you co-wrote a song, all writers must be registered with their respective PROs or royalties for their share won't flow.

Master vs Composition

Two Different Copyrights

Reference · Foundational Concept

Why this matters

Every song is two assets. The MASTER is the sound recording — owned by whoever paid for the studio (the artist if independent, the label if signed). The COMPOSITION is the underlying melody + lyrics — owned by the songwriter and (optionally) their publisher. Streaming royalties pay the master holder. Publishing royalties pay the composition holders.

  • Master rights = sound-recording royalties (streaming, sync of the recording, neighboring rights).
  • Composition rights = performance royalties (PRO), mechanical royalties (MLC), sync of the song.
  • If you self-record AND self-write, you own BOTH. If a label signs you, they typically take the master.
  • Per Songtrust: "Allowing a label to acquire publishing rights as part of a recording deal without negotiating them separately" is one of the most expensive mistakes a new artist can make.

Writer's Share Forever Yours

50/50 Split — Always

Reference · The Sacred Half

Why this matters

From Songtrust: "Publishing royalties split 50/50 between the writer's share (always goes to the songwriter directly via their PRO) and the publisher's share (goes to the publisher, who pays the songwriter per their deal)." The writer's share never leaves the songwriter — even in the worst deal you've ever seen, the writer's share still flows to you through ASCAP / BMI / SESAC.

  • Writer's share = 50% of all composition royalties, paid directly to the songwriter via their PRO.
  • Publisher's share = the OTHER 50%, paid to whoever holds the publishing rights (you, an admin, or a publisher).
  • Every "publishing deal" is really a negotiation about the publisher's share — never the writer's.
  • Co-writers split the writer's share by writing percentage. ALL co-writers must register with their PRO.

3 Deal Types

Admin · Co-Pub · Full Pub

Reference · The Decision Tree

Why this matters

Per Lawyerdrummer's industry-standard taxonomy:

  • Admin: "Songwriter retains 100% copyright ownership. Publisher administers collection globally and takes 10–25% commission during the deal term only."
  • Co-Publishing (per ASCAP): "Songwriter assigns 50% of their copyright to the publisher. Publisher collects its percentage from all songs created during the deal term — often for the life of the copyright (70+ years after death)."
  • Full Publishing: Publisher owns 100% of the copyright. Songwriter typically gets a larger advance + still receives writer's share via PRO. "Increasingly rare and generally unfavorable."
  • Independent / DIY: Skip the deal entirely. Pair PRO + a publishing administrator (Songtrust, TuneCore Publishing) and keep both halves yourself.
