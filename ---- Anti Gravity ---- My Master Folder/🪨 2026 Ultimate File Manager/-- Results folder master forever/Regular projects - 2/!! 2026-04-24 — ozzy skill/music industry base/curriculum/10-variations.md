# Music Industry — 10-Step Curriculum, 10 Variations
Source base: `music industry base/research/cats-1-13.js` + `cats-14-26.js` (26 categories total)
Tone: cross-cultural, multinational, common-man. Steps 1–8 are baby steps. Step 9 is "all comes together." Step 10 is "where do we go from here?"

---

## Variation 1 — Money-First
**Spine:** Follow every dollar a song earns from the listener's ear all the way to the artist's pocket.

| Step | Title | Source cat | One-sentence teach |
|------|-------|-----------|--------------------|
| 1 | Where the dollar starts | Cat 1 (Streaming) | A listener's monthly $10 subscription becomes a tiny per-stream payout (about $0.003–$0.008) shared across millions of artists. |
| 2 | Two dollars in every song | Cat 3 (How Royalties Work) | Every song actually earns two royalty streams — one for the recording (master) and one for the underlying composition (publishing). |
| 3 | The waterfall to your bank | Cat 1 (Streaming) + Cat 5 (Distribution) | Money flows DSP → distributor → label → artist, and at each pipe somebody takes a cut. |
| 4 | Your missing publishing money | Cat 3 (How Royalties Work) + Cat 17 (PROs) | Until you sign up with a PRO and the MLC, half your earnings sit in a holding pool with your name unlinked. |
| 5 | The license fee jackpot | Cat 4 (Sync / Music Licensing) | One TV ad placement can pay more than 5 million streams — and indie artists who own everything have the upper hand. |
| 6 | The merch table secret | Cat 21 (Merch & Fan Monetization) | A $30 t-shirt with $6 in cost beats almost any per-stream economics in the entire industry. |
| 7 | Live show math | Cat 13 (Live Performance & Touring) | After venue, gas, and lodging, the artist often nets $8–$15 from a $100 ticket — merch is what turns a tour profitable. |
| 8 | Hidden global money | Cat 26 (Global Music Landscape Charts) + Cat 17 (PROs) | Royalties earned in Germany, Japan, or Brazil only reach you if you've registered globally — otherwise the money disappears. |
| 9 | "All comes together" — Building your money map | (composite of Cats 1, 3, 4, 13, 21, 26) | A sustainable artist income is six small rivers (streams, mechanicals, performance, sync, live, merch) — not one big one. |
| 10 | Where do we go from here? | — | Pick one leaky pipe (PRO registration, MLC signup, merch margin, sync metadata) and plug it this month — the rest grow once the leaks stop. |

## Variation 2 — Ownership-First
**Spine:** What you own when the song is finished is more important than what you earn from it this year.

| Step | Title | Source cat | One-sentence teach |
|------|-------|-----------|--------------------|
| 1 | The song is two things | Cat 16 (Copyright & Ownership) | Every recorded song contains two separate copyrights — the composition (melody + lyrics) and the master (the recording). |
| 2 | Copyright starts the moment you hit record | Cat 16 (Copyright & Ownership) | You own a copyright as soon as the song is fixed in any form — registration just gives you the right to enforce it in court. |
| 3 | Split sheets before the session ends | Cat 16 (Copyright & Ownership) | Write down who owns what percentage of the song before anyone leaves the studio — verbal deals collapse the moment money shows up. |
| 4 | Who paid for the recording owns it | Cat 7 (Record Labels) + Cat 18 (Production Costs) | Whoever funds the recording typically owns the master — that's why labels keep masters and indie artists who self-fund don't. |
| 5 | Watch the work-for-hire trap | Cat 16 (Copyright & Ownership) + Cat 18 (Production Costs) | A producer or session player who signs "work for hire" gives up all copyright in their contribution forever. |
| 6 | Admin vs. co-pub vs. full publishing | Cat 6 (Music Publishing) | Admin = you keep ownership and pay 10–25% commission; co-pub = you give up half forever; full publishing = you give up everything. |
| 7 | Licensing instead of selling | Cat 7 (Record Labels) | A licensing deal hands the label your masters for 5–7 years and then returns them — far better than a traditional ownership grab. |
| 8 | Reversion and termination rights | Cat 15 (Music Contracts) + Cat 16 (Copyright & Ownership) | U.S. law lets you reclaim transferred copyrights at year 35 — but only if a notice is filed in the open window. |
| 9 | "All comes together" — Owning your catalog | (composite of Cats 6, 7, 15, 16) | An artist who owns their masters AND their publishing controls every future income stream the song will ever earn. |
| 10 | Where do we go from here? | — | Audit every contract and split sheet you've ever signed — anything murky becomes a lawsuit or a lost royalty in five years. |

## Variation 3 — Audience-First
**Spine:** Build a real audience that loves you before you spend a single dollar chasing a deal.

| Step | Title | Source cat | One-sentence teach |
|------|-------|-----------|--------------------|
| 1 | A fan beats a follower | Cat 9 (Artist Branding) | The top 1–5% of fans (the superfans) generate most of an artist's lifetime revenue — broad casual reach is the cheap version. |
| 2 | Pick one platform and post like a human | Cat 2 (Social Media) | TikTok rewards unpolished real moments more than pretty studio shots — 84% of Billboard Global 200 hits in 2024 broke there first. |
| 3 | Posting cadence is the algorithm | Cat 2 (Social Media) | Algorithms reward 3–5 posts a week on a steady schedule — going dark for two weeks resets your reach to zero. |
| 4 | Show the human, not just the music | Cat 9 (Artist Branding) | Fans bond with artists through "knowing them" — process, opinions, and behind-the-scenes content build the parasocial trust that drives loyalty. |
| 5 | The email list you actually own | Cat 10 (Music Marketing & Campaigns) | Email converts 5–10x better than social and is the only audience platform algorithm changes can't take from you. |
| 6 | Local fans are where it starts | Cat 13 (Live Performance & Touring) | Build draw in your home city before touring — empty rooms in unfamiliar markets damage your reputation with venues. |
| 7 | Save rate beats stream count | Cat 11 (Playlist Pitching) | A high save-to-stream ratio tells DSP algorithms you have real fans, which unlocks Discover Weekly and Release Radar. |
| 8 | Press is for credibility, not streams | Cat 12 (Music PR & Press) + Cat 23 (Music Blogs) | Blog coverage signals "this artist is worth paying attention to" — the streaming bump is small, the industry credibility is large. |
| 9 | "All comes together" — The fan flywheel | (composite of Cats 2, 9, 10, 11, 13) | Real fans → high save rate → algorithmic lift → editorial playlists → industry interest, in that exact order. |
| 10 | Where do we go from here? | — | Write down the names and locations of your first 100 superfans — once you can picture them, every business decision gets easier. |

## Variation 4 — Operator-First
**Spine:** Learn who actually does each job in the music industry machine, and you'll never get bullied by the wrong title again.

| Step | Title | Source cat | One-sentence teach |
|------|-------|-----------|--------------------|
| 1 | Distributor — your delivery driver | Cat 5 (Music Distribution) | A distributor moves your audio to all the streaming platforms; they don't market it, they don't own it, they're the freight company. |
| 2 | Label — the bank with opinions | Cat 7 (Record Labels) | A label puts up the marketing money in exchange for ownership and a royalty share — its true value is capital + radio + relationships. |
| 3 | Publisher — protector of the song | Cat 6 (Music Publishing) | A publisher registers your composition globally and chases every dollar the melody and lyrics earn, separate from the recording. |
| 4 | PRO — the radio police | Cat 17 (PROs) | ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, SOCAN, GEMA, JASRAC etc. monitor public performances worldwide and pay the writer's share directly to the songwriter. |
| 5 | Manager — strategy and gatekeeper | Cat 8 (Artist Management) | A manager runs the long game (15–20% of gross) — they don't book shows or write contracts, they direct everyone who does. |
| 6 | Booking agent — the show closer | Cat 14 (Booking Agents & Venues) | A booking agent (10–15% of show fees) negotiates live deals with promoters and venues; it's a separate, often state-licensed role from manager. |
| 7 | Entertainment attorney — your personal goalie | Cat 24 (Building Your Team) | A music-specific attorney ($350–$500/hour) catches contract traps a general lawyer will miss every single time. |
| 8 | Producer — the build captain | Cat 18 (Music Production Costs) | A producer can be paid by points, advance + points, or flat buyout — each structure changes who owns what at the end. |
| 9 | "All comes together" — The team chart | (composite of Cats 5, 6, 7, 8, 14, 17, 18, 24) | The artist sits in the center; every other operator has one job, one fee, and one boundary — confusion is the only thing that kills the system. |
| 10 | Where do we go from here? | — | Draw your current team chart — every empty slot is an opportunity, every overlapping slot is a conflict waiting to happen. |

| 2 | The U.S. is just one stop | Cat 26 (Global Music Landscape) | The U.S. is the biggest single market (31.5%) but Japan, the UK, Germany, China, France, Korea, Brazil, and Mexico together are bigger. |
| 3 | Different platforms in different places | Cat 5 (Distribution) + Cat 26 (Global Music) | Spotify dominates Western markets, but China runs on QQ Music and NetEase, Korea on Melon, Japan on physical and Line Music. |
| 4 | Where the growth actually is | Cat 26 (Global Music Landscape) | Sub-Saharan Africa (+24% YoY), Latin America (+19%), and Southeast Asia (+15%) are growing 2–3x faster than the U.S. |
| 5 | Foreign royalties don't show up by themselves | Cat 6 (Music Publishing) + Cat 17 (PROs) | Without sub-publishing or admin coverage, the money you earn in Germany, France, or Japan sits uncollected forever. |
| 6 | Neighboring rights — extra money outside the U.S. | Cat 22 (Radio) + Cat 26 (Global Music) | Most of the world pays the recording artist when their song plays on radio; the U.S. is the unique outlier that doesn't. |
| 7 | Cross-cultural collaboration is the unlock | Cat 26 (Global Music) + Cat 2 (Social Media) | A Spanish-language feature or an Afrobeats remix opens entire regional audiences that don't speak your first language. |
| 8 | Local strategy beats global broadcast | Cat 10 (Marketing) + Cat 26 (Global Music) | A targeted Mexico or Indonesia campaign gets more traction than a worldwide blast that lands nowhere in particular. |
| 9 | "All comes together" — The global passport | (composite of Cats 5, 6, 17, 22, 26) | An artist with global publishing admin + neighboring rights + region-aware DSP strategy + one cross-cultural collab is positioned for global income. |

## Variation 7 — Legal-First
**Spine:** Contracts and copyright are the bedrock — get the paperwork right and the rest of the career can actually be built on it.

| Step | Title | Source cat | One-sentence teach |
|------|-------|-----------|--------------------|
| 1 | Two copyrights, one song | Cat 16 (Copyright & Ownership) | Every recorded song has a composition copyright AND a sound recording copyright — they live in different houses with different keys. |
| 2 | Register before you have a problem | Cat 16 (Copyright & Ownership) | Registration in the U.S. costs $45–$65 per work and unlocks statutory damages of $30K–$150K per infringement. |
| 3 | Recoupment — the most misunderstood word | Cat 7 (Record Labels) + Cat 15 (Music Contracts) | A label "advance" is a loan repaid from your royalty share — you can go platinum and still be unrecouped on paper. |
| 4 | Cross-collateralization is a trap | Cat 15 (Music Contracts) | Labels can roll Album 1's debt onto Album 2's earnings — even a hit second album can produce zero artist income. |
| 5 | The 360 deal grabs everything | Cat 7 (Record Labels) + Cat 15 (Music Contracts) | Some labels claim 15–25% of touring, merch, sync, and brand deals — never sign one without expense caps and a percentage floor. |
| 6 | Option clauses lock you in for albums you haven't made | Cat 15 (Music Contracts) | Standard major label deals include 3–7 future album options exercisable only by the label — you can be tied up for a decade. |
| 7 | Sunset clause for managers | Cat 8 (Artist Management) | Without a sunset clause, a former manager keeps collecting full commission on deals they made — sometimes for life. |
| 8 | Entertainment attorney is non-negotiable | Cat 24 (Building Your Team) | A $500–$2,000 attorney review before signing is trivial compared to a 35-year unfavorable contract. |
| 9 | "All comes together" — Reading any music contract in plain English | (composite of Cats 6, 7, 8, 15, 16, 24) | Every clause you understand is a clause that can't surprise you — the goal is contract literacy, not paranoia. |
| 10 | Where do we go from here? | — | Build a relationship with one music attorney now, before you need one — the time to find your goalie is before the shootout, not during. |

| Step | Title | Source cat | One-sentence teach |
|------|-------|-----------|--------------------|

| 2 | A $25/year flat-fee distributor delivers globally | Cat 5 (Music Distribution) | DistroKid, TuneCore, CD Baby, and UnitedMasters move your music to every major DSP with no label needed. |
| 3 | Own your masters by paying for the recording | Cat 16 (Copyright & Ownership) + Cat 18 (Production Costs) | Whoever funds the recording owns the master — self-funding keeps that ownership in your hands forever. |
| 4 | Use a publishing admin, not a publisher | Cat 6 (Music Publishing) | Songtrust, TuneCore Publishing, or DistroKid Songwriting collect global publishing for 10–25% with no ownership grab. |
| 5 | Build the audience before chasing the deal | Cat 2 (Social Media) + Cat 9 (Artist Branding) | A consistent posting + branding system on TikTok/IG/YT is the indie artist's free marketing department. |

| 7 | Direct-to-fan is the highest margin | Cat 21 (Merch) | Bandcamp, Shopify, and Patreon let you keep 80–95% of merch and subscription revenue — labels and venues take far more. |
| 8 | Sync is a real income stream for indies | Cat 4 (Sync Licensing) | Music supervisors prefer "one-stop" tracks (one party owns master + publishing) — that's exactly what an indie artist offers. |
| 9 | "All comes together" — Running your own label | (composite of Cats 2, 5, 6, 9, 11, 16, 18, 21) | An indie artist with all eight pieces working in sync IS a small label — minus the contract that gives the masters away. |

## Variation 9 — Touring-First
**Spine:** Live performance is where music actually meets people — the rest of the industry exists to feed and support it.

| Step | Title | Source cat | One-sentence teach |
|------|-------|-----------|--------------------|
| 1 | Local first, regional second, national last | Cat 13 (Live Performance) | Every level of touring requires draw earned at the level below — skipping steps means empty rooms and burned bridges. |
| 2 | Door deal vs. guarantee vs. hybrid | Cat 13 (Live Performance) + Cat 14 (Booking Agents) | A "guarantee vs. door split (whichever is greater)" is the artist-protective structure once draw is proven. |
| 3 | Routing is profit | Cat 13 (Live Performance) | Strategic geographic routing cuts travel costs 20–40% — clustering shows beats chasing whoever offered first. |
| 4 | Venue tiers and what each pays | Cat 14 (Booking Agents & Venues) | Club ($200–$5K), theater ($5K–$75K), arena ($50K–$500K+) — your show fee follows your provable ticket draw. |
| 5 | Booking agents enter when you book 20+ shows/year | Cat 14 (Booking Agents) + Cat 24 (Building Team) | Booking agents take 10–15% of show fees and bring leverage with promoters — premature signing wastes both sides' time. |
| 6 | Merch is the actual profit center | Cat 21 (Merch) + Cat 13 (Live Performance) | Most touring artists earn more from a sold-out merch table than from their show fee — pricing and presentation matter as much as the music. |
| 7 | Email + setlist registration = future money | Cat 10 (Marketing) + Cat 17 (PROs) | Every show is a chance to grow your email list AND submit setlists to your PRO for live performance royalties. |
| 8 | Treat early tours as marketing, not income | Cat 13 (Live Performance) | Most independent artists lose money on the first national tour — that loss is the marketing budget for the next one. |
| 9 | "All comes together" — The tour as a self-contained business | (composite of Cats 10, 13, 14, 17, 21, 24) | Routing + deal structure + merch + email + PRO setlists + booking relationships turn each show into a four-way revenue event. |
| 10 | Where do we go from here? | — | Pick the next 5 cities you can drive between in 7 days, book one show in each, and let the math of a real route teach you the industry. |

## Variation 10 — Hybrid-Modern (2026)
**Spine:** The 2026 artist is independent by default, partners selectively, uses AI as a workforce, and thinks globally from day one.

| Step | Title | Source cat | One-sentence teach |
|------|-------|-----------|--------------------|
| 1 | Independent is the new default | Cat 5 (Distribution) + Cat 7 (Record Labels) | Distribution and recording are now affordable and self-serve — artists sign deals in 2026 only when capital, radio, or relationships are needed. |
| 2 | AI is a workflow tool, not a creator | Cat 25 (AI in Music) | iZotope, LALAL, LANDR, and Suno-as-sketchpad are normal in 2026 — but commercial releases still need substantive human authorship to be copyrightable. |
| 3 | Cross-platform content from one shoot | Cat 2 (Social Media) + Cat 20 (Music Videos) | One music video shoot now feeds horizontal YouTube, vertical TikTok/Reels, square IG, and 15-second teaser cuts. |
| 4 | Streaming + sync + merch + live = four-rivers income | Cat 1 + Cat 4 + Cat 13 + Cat 21 | No single income stream pays a sustainable career — modern artists build four small rivers, not one big one. |
| 5 | Selective partnerships, not full surrender | Cat 7 (Record Labels) + Cat 6 (Music Publishing) | Licensing deals (5–7 years, masters revert) and admin publishing deals (you keep ownership) are the modern partnerships. |
| 6 | Spatial audio and AI metadata | Cat 19 (Mixing & Mastering) + Cat 25 (AI in Music) | Apple Music's spatial section favors Atmos masters, and supervisors search via AI on metadata — both are now baseline expectations. |
| 7 | Global from release day one | Cat 26 (Global Music) + Cat 5 (Distribution) | A 2026 release simultaneously hits 180+ markets — preparing region-aware metadata and one cross-cultural collaboration multiplies reach. |
| 8 | Superfan economy on top of broad reach | Cat 9 (Branding) + Cat 21 (Merch & Fan Monetization) | Patreon, Discord, Bandcamp, and tiered fan clubs turn the loyal 1–5% of fans into recurring monthly income. |
| 9 | "All comes together" — The 2026 artist's operating system | (composite of Cats 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 13, 19, 20, 21, 25, 26) | Independent base + AI-augmented production + global release + superfan layer + selective partners = the modern, sustainable artist. |
| 10 | Where do we go from here? | — | Audit your stack against the eight pieces above — the weakest piece, fixed first, lifts every other piece automatically. |
