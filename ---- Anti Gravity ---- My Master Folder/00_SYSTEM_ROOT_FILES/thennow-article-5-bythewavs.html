Let's Talk About What THENNOW Is Actually Doing Sonically

The thing that separates THENNOW's production from a lot of what's circulating in underground bass right now is the discipline in the mix. Bass music — whether you're talking riddim, wave, tearout, or any of the heavier sub-genres — is brutally unforgiving to sloppiness in the low end. You can get away with a mediocre arrangement if the sub hits right. You can survive a basic synth design if the percussion snaps. But when everything hits at once and it still breathes? That's engineering. That's not a preset doing the work — that's someone who understands gain staging, frequency separation, and how to make 50Hz and 8kHz coexist without either one suffering.

Both new tracks operate in that space. Heavy percussion that punches with impact rather than just volume. Sound design that's sharp without being brittle — you know the difference, that ear-fatiguing harshness that comes from bad limiting or over-processed highs. None of that here. The clarity in these mixes is a technical flex, and it's the kind of thing that will hit harder on a proper system than it does on laptop speakers.

Thick, dense production that still feels immediate. That balance is genuinely difficult to achieve. Most producers landing on the heavy end of underground bass either sacrifice the detail for the weight, or they keep the detail and the track never really breathes. THENNOW has found the fulcrum point.

The Remix Is the One That Will Get People Talking

TAKE vip (Supertask – Get to You Remix) takes Supertask and Saint Sinner's "Get to You" — which already had emotional depth in its original form — and runs it through something you might loosely describe as wave-adjacent, except it's heavier and meaner than most of what gets that label. The atmospherics are warped and disorienting in a way that feels deliberate rather than random. There's a particular technique that good wave-adjacent producers use where they push a pad or vocal through modulation and pitch-shifting until it retains just enough of its original identity to feel familiar while becoming almost alien — and that's exactly what's happening here to the vocal.

Underneath that warped dreamscape, the low-end is doing something more aggressive. It snarls. That's the only accurate word for it. Not the smooth sub roll of something like melodic dubstep, not the mechanical grind of riddim — something in between that has teeth. The emotional core of the Supertask original was warmth and longing. THENNOW has taken that and made it nocturnal, a little threatening, the kind of thing you'd hear at 3 a.m. in a basement when the night has gotten weird in the best way.

For listeners in the scene, the reference points are there without being obvious. This isn't a copycat of any particular sub-genre — it's someone who has absorbed the influences and built something that belongs to its own lane.
