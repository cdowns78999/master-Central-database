# Phase 2: Spotify Deep Dive (The Algorithmic Landscape)

This phase instructs the artist on the internal mechanics of Spotify, detailing how different playlist types drive growth, which data signals the algorithm prioritizes, and how to protect the integrity of their data stream from fraudulent activity.

## Class 201: Understanding Playlisting: Start to Finish

**Class Description**
This class provides a comprehensive overview of the three critical playlist categories that govern music discovery on Spotify: **Editorial** (human-curated), **Algorithmic** (personalized), and **User-Generated** (listener-created). Mastering the mechanics of how to engage with each type is the first step toward sustained stream growth.

**Success Steps / Tool / Guide (Closed Loop)**

**Playlist Type Delineator & Pitch Navigator:** A visual guide that clearly separates the three types of playlists, explaining their source and utility. This tool links directly to the **Spotify for Artists Pitching Tool** and provides information on accessing the **Marquee** campaign feature.

**All the Details You Know I Want**

*   **Problem Solved:** How editorial, algorithmic, and user playlists work together to maximize stream generation and fan acquisition.
*   **Success Steps/Tool Implementation:**
    *   **Editorial Playlists:** These are curated by human editors. Submission must be made via the Pitch Tool in Spotify for Artists at least **seven days in advance**. Spotify editors look for momentum and promotional plans when considering inclusion.
    *   **Algorithmic Playlists:** These include **Release Radar**, **Discover Weekly**, and **Radio**. Release Radar is guaranteed for followers if the track is pitched at least seven days prior. Algorithmic playlists account for a significant portion of new artist discovery.
    *   **Marquee:** This is a promotional tool that alerts existing listeners and followers about new releases and is available for artists in the US through Spotify for Artists.
    *   **User Playlists:** These are created by individuals. Adding music to real, active user playlists sends positive signals to the Spotify algorithm.

## Class 202: Playlisting – University Studies (Deep Understanding)

**Class Description**
This highly analytical class dissects the algorithmic signals that Spotify uses to determine a song's overall **Popularity Score**. Success is driven by high-quality data—measured by metrics like **Save Rate** and **Skip Rate**—which, when optimized, can trigger inclusion in the powerful algorithmic playlists like Discover Weekly and Radio. This course teaches artists how to measure and influence these crucial metrics.

**Success Steps / Tool / Guide (Closed Loop)**

**Algorithmic Signal Tracker:** A checklist and data logging tool for monitoring the key metrics that influence the Popularity Score (which can be found using third-party tools like Musicsstacks.com). This tool tracks **Save Rate** and encourages the strategy of **Front-Loading Budget** to maximize score improvements immediately upon release.

**All the Details You Know I Want**

*   **Problem Solved:** Understanding metadata, save rate, skip rate, listener behavior, and release timing to trigger algorithmic playlists.
*   **Success Steps/Tool Implementation:**
    *   **Save Rate is Key:** Spotify cares deeply about **saves**, listing it as almost as important as streams and listeners in the data reporting. When a listener saves a song to their library, they are much more likely to listen to it long-term.
    *   **Popularity Score Threshold:** If a song achieves a Popularity Score of about **30%**, it is likely to be included on Discover Weekly.
    *   **Front-Load Promotion:** Spending the majority of the budget and promotional effort in the **first three days** of release is advised to quickly boost the Popularity Score and ride the resulting algorithmic wave. This is the most crucial time to get a lot of streams and saves.
    *   **Metadata Pitch:** The pitch submitted to Spotify must include specific genres, instrumentation, collaborators, and promotional plans to help the platform categorize the music correctly.

## Class 203: Spotify Bots: Let’s Bust Myths and Clear the Air

**Class Description**
This critical class focuses on maintaining the integrity of an artist's data pool. It explains the financial and career risks associated with purchasing **fake streams, bots, or low-quality placements** (artificial streaming). Artists learn to identify fraudulent services and how to vet legitimate curators who rely on human review instead of guaranteed placement metrics.

**Success Steps / Tool / Guide (Closed Loop)**

**Fraud Avoidance Checklist & Data Health Monitor:** A warning screen listing indicators of fraudulent activity and detailing the consequences of data contamination. The **Data Health Monitor** highlights the **Fans Also Like** section (visible in Spotify for Artists) as a crucial check: if irrelevant artists appear there, it signals that the algorithm has been skewed by bad data. This tool also provides links to legitimate submission platforms.

**All the Details You Know I Want**

*   **Problem Solved:** How to identify fake activity, how to avoid it, and how Spotify detects music that does not reflect genuine user listening intent.
*   **Success Steps/Tool Implementation:**
    *   **Data Contamination:** Buying streams or getting placed on bot-filled user-generated playlists **contaminates the listener data**, which ruins the algorithm's ability to accurately target the artist's real audience.
    *   **Consequences:** If the data is ruined, the algorithm will serve the music to the wrong people, potentially leading to a high skip rate (a negative signal) and listing irrelevant artists in the "Fans Also Like" section.
    *   **Vetting Curators:** Avoid services that promise a flat rate for guaranteed high follower counts. Use recognized, legitimate platforms like **SubmitHub** or **Playlist Push**, which give artists access to individual human curators.
    *   **Cost of Legitimate Services:** Be aware that platforms like Playlist Push can be expensive compared to their competitors.
