From Founding to Death

An Entire Religion Built in Just Over a Decade

Between the church's founding in April 1830 and Joseph Smith's death in June 1844, Mormonism went from 6 members in a farmhouse to 26,000+ followers, built an entire city (Nauvoo, Illinois — population ~12,000), constructed a massive temple, established a militia, and Smith even ran for President of the United States. No religious movement in American history scaled this fast.

"No man knows my history." — Joseph Smith Jr., 1844
The World Joseph Smith Was Born Into
☯

The Second Great Awakening

The early 1800s in upstate New York was an absolute frenzy of religious revival. So many preachers and new movements swept through the area that it earned the nickname "The Burned-over District" — the spiritual fire had passed through so many times there was nobody left to convert. Methodists, Baptists, Presbyterians, and dozens of smaller sects were all competing for souls. This was the world young Joseph grew up in — every denomination claiming to be the one true church.

🌱

The Smith Family

Joseph's father, Joseph Smith Sr., was a struggling farmer who lost the family savings in a ginseng export scam, then lost their Vermont farm to repeated crop failures. His mother, Lucy Mack Smith, came from a family connected to New England Congregationalism. The family was deeply involved in folk religious practices common to rural New England — treasure-digging, divining rods, and "seer stones" used to find buried objects. Joseph Jr. himself was hired as a treasure-seeker and used a seer stone placed in a hat to "see" things.

📚

Why This Matters

Understanding the context is everything. Joseph Smith didn't emerge from a vacuum — he was a product of a culture that was actively looking for new revelation. Folk magic and visionary religion were mainstream in this time and place. The line between "religious seer" and "treasure digger" was blurry. Smith's early use of seer stones (which he'd later use to translate the Book of Mormon) connects directly to his family's folk magic traditions. Scholars see continuity; the church sees a young prophet being prepared. Both are looking at the same facts.

The Complete Timeline
Spring 1820 — The First Vision

God the Father & Jesus Christ Appear to a 14-Year-Old

According to Joseph Smith's account, he was confused by all the competing churches and went into a grove of trees near his family's farm to pray and ask God which church was right. What happened next became the foundational event of Mormonism: he claimed that God the Father and Jesus Christ appeared to him as two separate, physical beings and told him that none of the existing churches were correct, and that the fullness of the gospel would be made known to him in time.

LDS Church Narrative
This is the foundational theophany — the moment God chose Joseph as his prophet to restore the true church. The vision established that God and Jesus are separate beings with glorified physical bodies, which is a core LDS doctrine that separates them from mainstream Christianity's Trinity.
Historical / Scholarly View
The earliest written account of this vision dates to 1832, twelve years after it allegedly occurred. Smith wrote at least four different versions between 1832 and 1842, with significant variations (in some, only one being appears; in the earliest, there's no mention of being told all churches are wrong). No contemporary documentation exists from 1820. Most scholars note this was not publicly taught in the early church — it didn't become a central narrative until the 1840s.
📍 Sacred Grove, near Palmyra, New York
Key figure: Joseph Smith Jr. (age 14)
September 21, 1823 — The Angel Moroni Visits

An Angel Reveals the Location of Ancient Golden Plates

Three years after the First Vision, Smith said the angel Moroni appeared in his bedroom, told him about a set of golden plates buried in a nearby hill that contained the record of ancient peoples who lived in the Americas. Moroni was said to be the last surviving member of these people — a Nephite — and had buried the plates around 421 AD. Smith was told he would be the one to translate them. The angel visited him three times that night, repeating the same message. Smith went to the hill the next day and found the plates in a stone box, but was told he couldn't take them yet — he had to return every year for four years to receive further instruction.

📍 Hill Cumorah, near Palmyra, New York
Key figures: Joseph Smith Jr. (age 17), the Angel Moroni
1824–1826 — The Waiting Years

Treasure Digging, a Trial, and Yearly Visits to the Hill

During the four-year waiting period, Smith continued working as a treasure digger and seer-for-hire, using his seer stone to help people locate buried treasure (a common practice in the region). In 1826, he was brought to trial in Bainbridge, New York, charged as a "disorderly person and impostor" for his treasure-seeking activities. Court records from this trial survive and confirm Smith was using a seer stone in a hat to locate treasures. He also met Emma Hale during this period while boarding at her father's home. Her father, Isaac Hale, refused to let them marry because of Smith's treasure-digging reputation, so they eloped on January 18, 1827.

📍 South Bainbridge, New York / Harmony, Pennsylvania
Key figures: Joseph Smith Jr., Emma Hale, Josiah Stowell (employer), Isaac Hale
September 22, 1827 — Smith Retrieves the Golden Plates

After Four Years of Annual Visits, He Finally Takes the Plates Home

On the fall equinox of 1827 — exactly four years after Moroni's first visit — Smith went to Hill Cumorah and retrieved the golden plates. He described them as being about 6 inches wide, 8 inches long, and 6 inches thick, bound together with three large rings, weighing roughly 40-60 pounds, and inscribed with "reformed Egyptian" characters. Along with the plates, he received two stones set in silver bows and attached to a breastplate — the Urim and Thummim — which were to be used for translation. Smith never publicly displayed the plates but showed them to select witnesses who later signed testimonies.

📍 Hill Cumorah → Smith farm, Manchester, NY
Key figures: Joseph Smith Jr., Emma Smith, Joseph Smith Sr.
December 1827 – June 1829 — The Translation

Translating the Book of Mormon — 531 Pages in ~65 Working Days

The translation process is one of the most debated aspects of the entire story. Smith and Emma moved to Harmony, Pennsylvania to escape local harassment. Initially, Martin Harris served as scribe. Harris's wife Lucy was skeptical, and when Harris took 116 translated manuscript pages home to show her, they were lost (possibly destroyed by Lucy). Smith was devastated and the translation stopped for months.

Translation resumed in April 1829 with a new scribe: Oliver Cowdery, a 22-year-old schoolteacher. Between April 7 and late June 1829, they produced the entire Book of Mormon — 531 printed pages in roughly 65 working days. That's about 8 pages per day of original text.

LDS Church Narrative
Smith translated by the "gift and power of God," using the Urim and Thummim and later his seer stone. The speed of translation is seen as evidence of divine aid — no 23-year-old with a 3rd-grade education could produce a 531-page book of complex narrative, theology, and prophecy that fast without help.
Historical / Scholarly View
Eyewitness accounts (including Emma Smith and David Whitmer) describe Smith placing a seer stone in a hat, pressing his face into the hat to block light, and reading off the translated words as they appeared on the stone. The golden plates were often not even in the room — sometimes covered by a cloth or left in the woods. This has led to significant debate about the role of the physical plates in the process. Sources like "View of the Hebrews" (1823), the KJV Bible, and contemporary ideas about Native Americans as lost Israelites have been proposed as influences.
📍 Harmony, PA → Fayette, NY (Whitmer farm)
Key figures: Joseph Smith, Emma Smith, Martin Harris, Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer
June 1829 — The Witnesses

Three Witnesses & Eight Witnesses See the Plates

Before publication, Smith arranged for witnesses to validate the plates. Three Witnesses (Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer, Martin Harris) testified that an angel showed them the plates and they heard God's voice confirm the translation. Eight Witnesses (members of the Whitmer and Smith families) said they physically handled the plates — hefted them, turned the pages, and saw the engravings. Their testimonies were printed in every copy of the Book of Mormon. Notably, all three of the Three Witnesses later left the church at various points (Harris and Cowdery returned; Whitmer never did), but none of them ever recanted their testimony about seeing the plates, even under intense social pressure.

📍 Fayette, New York
Three Witnesses: Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer, Martin Harris / Eight Witnesses: Christian Whitmer, Jacob Whitmer, Peter Whitmer Jr., John Whitmer, Hiram Page, Joseph Smith Sr., Hyrum Smith, Samuel Smith
March 26, 1830 — The Book of Mormon Published

5,000 Copies Roll Off the Press

The Book of Mormon was published by E.B. Grandin in Palmyra, New York. Martin Harris mortgaged his farm for $3,000 (about $100,000+ today) to finance the printing. The book tells the story of two ancient Israelite civilizations that supposedly migrated to the Americas — the Jaredites (around the Tower of Babel era) and the family of Lehi (around 600 BC). It describes wars, prophets, and culminates in Jesus Christ visiting the Americas after his resurrection. The book was met with immediate controversy — local newspapers had been mocking the project for years.

📍 Palmyra, New York (Grandin Print Shop)
Key figures: Joseph Smith, Martin Harris (financier), E.B. Grandin (printer)
April 6, 1830 — The Church is Founded

"The Church of Christ" Officially Organized

With six founding members (the legal minimum under New York law), the church was organized at the Peter Whitmer Sr. farm in Fayette, Seneca County, New York. Originally called "The Church of Christ," it was later renamed to "The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints" in 1838. Joseph Smith was sustained as "First Elder." The church grew rapidly through missionary work — by the end of 1830, they had several hundred members. Converts were drawn by the Book of Mormon, personal testimonies, and the promise of living prophets and continuing revelation.

📍 Fayette, Seneca County, New York
Six founding members: Joseph Smith Jr., Oliver Cowdery, Hyrum Smith, Peter Whitmer Jr., Samuel Smith, David Whitmer
January 1831 – 1837 — The Kirtland Era

Moving to Ohio, Building a Temple, and Financial Collapse

Smith received a revelation to gather the saints in Kirtland, Ohio, where a large group of converts led by Sidney Rigdon (a former Campbellite preacher) were waiting. This was the church's first major headquarters. Major developments: Smith produced several other scriptural works including the Book of Moses, the Book of Abraham (translated from Egyptian papyri he purchased), and the Doctrine and Covenants (a collection of his revelations). The saints built the Kirtland Temple (dedicated March 27, 1836) — the first LDS temple, where members reported visions and speaking in tongues during the dedication.

The era ended in disaster: Smith established the Kirtland Safety Society, a quasi-bank, in 1837. It collapsed within months due to the Panic of 1837, and members lost their savings. Nearly a third of the church leadership defected, including several apostles. Smith and Rigdon fled Kirtland in January 1838 to escape arrest for bank fraud charges.

📍 Kirtland, Ohio
Key figures: Joseph Smith, Sidney Rigdon, Oliver Cowdery, the Twelve Apostles (organized 1835)
1838 — The Missouri War

Extermination Order, Massacres, and Imprisonment

While Kirtland was the spiritual headquarters, Smith had simultaneously designated Independence, Missouri as the site for "Zion" — the literal gathering place of God's people. Mormons had been moving to Missouri since 1831, and tensions with existing settlers explated into open violence. Mormons voted as a bloc, opposed slavery (Missouri was a slave state), and local settlers feared being politically overwhelmed.

In 1838, things reached a crisis: the Danite militia (a secretive Mormon paramilitary group) formed, armed clashes broke out, and on October 27, 1838, Missouri Governor Lilburn Boggs issued Executive Order 44 — the infamous "Extermination Order" — declaring that Mormons must be "treated as enemies" and "exterminated or driven from the state." Three days later, a mob attacked the Mormon settlement at Haun's Mill, killing 17 men and boys. Smith surrendered and was imprisoned in Liberty Jail for five months. The Mormons — around 8,000 to 10,000 of them — were expelled from Missouri in the dead of winter.

📍 Far West, Missouri / Independence, MO / Haun's Mill / Liberty Jail
Key figures: Joseph Smith, Sidney Rigdon, Gov. Lilburn Boggs, Sampson Avard (Danite leader), Hyrum Smith
1839 – 1844 — The Nauvoo Era

Building a City, a Temple, a Militia, and Running for President

After escaping or being released from Missouri, Smith led his followers to Commerce, Illinois, which he renamed Nauvoo (Hebrew for "beautiful"). He secured an incredibly powerful city charter from the Illinois legislature that gave Nauvoo its own courts, militia (the Nauvoo Legion, ~5,000 strong — nearly half the size of the U.S. Army at the time), and near-autonomy from state law.

Nauvoo grew to ~12,000 residents by 1844, rivaling Chicago in size. Smith introduced some of his most controversial and significant doctrines during this period: baptism for the dead (performing baptisms on behalf of deceased ancestors), the endowment ceremony (sacred temple rituals influenced by Freemasonry), celestial marriage (eternal marriage, including plural marriage / polygamy), and the King Follett Discourse (April 1844) — a radical sermon declaring that God was once a man and that humans can become gods ("As man now is, God once was; as God now is, man may be").

📍 Nauvoo, Illinois
Key figures: Joseph Smith, Emma Smith, Hyrum Smith, Brigham Young, John C. Bennett, William Law
1841 – 1844 — Polygamy

The Secret That Would Tear Everything Apart

This is the single most controversial chapter of Smith's life. Historical evidence now confirms that Smith married between 30 and 40 women, some as young as 14 (Helen Mar Kimball), and some who were already married to other living men (polyandry). These marriages were conducted in secret, and Smith publicly denied the practice even as he was privately performing it. Emma Smith was devastated and frequently opposed the practice, though she briefly accepted a few of the marriages.

The secrecy created enormous internal conflict. When William Law, a member of the First Presidency (essentially #2 in the church), learned about the extent of polygamy and that Smith had approached his wife, he broke away and published the Nauvoo Expositor newspaper on June 7, 1844 — a single issue that exposed polygamy and accused Smith of becoming a theocratic tyrant. Smith, as mayor, ordered the printing press destroyed. This act would directly lead to his death.

LDS Church Narrative (Modern)
The LDS Church formally acknowledged Smith's polygamy in a 2014 essay series on their website (Gospel Topics Essays). They frame it as a difficult but divinely commanded practice that Smith was reluctant to implement. The church discontinued polygamy in 1890 under President Wilford Woodruff.
Historical / Scholarly View
Historian Todd Compton's research ("In Sacred Loneliness") documents 33 confirmed wives. The youngest was 14. Eleven were already married to other men. The secrecy, the public denials, and the power dynamics involved (some women were told their eternal salvation depended on accepting) are deeply problematic by any modern standard. This is the area where the historical record most sharply diverges from the church's traditional narrative.
📍 Nauvoo, Illinois
Key figures: Joseph Smith, Emma Smith, William Law, Helen Mar Kimball, Fanny Alger (possibly the first plural wife, ~1833)
January – June 1844 — The Presidential Campaign

Joseph Smith Runs for President of the United States

In January 1844, Smith announced his candidacy for President of the United States. His platform included: abolishing slavery by 1850 (using federal funds to compensate slaveholders), reducing the size of Congress by two-thirds, establishing a national bank, annexing Texas and Oregon, and prison reform. He sent hundreds of missionaries across the country to campaign for him. His political organization, the Council of Fifty, also secretly ordained him "King of the Kingdom of God" — a theocratic title that would become explosive if discovered. The campaign ended with his death.

📍 Nauvoo, Illinois (campaign headquarters)
Key figures: Joseph Smith, the Council of Fifty, the Quorum of the Twelve (serving as campaign organizers)
June 7 – June 27, 1844 — The Final Days

The Expositor, the Arrest, and Carthage Jail

June 7: The Nauvoo Expositor publishes its first (and only) issue, exposing polygamy and accusing Smith of theocratic ambitions. June 10: The Nauvoo city council declares the Expositor a "public nuisance" and Smith orders the printing press destroyed. June 12: Smith is charged with inciting a riot. He declares martial law in Nauvoo. June 18: Smith briefly flees across the Mississippi but returns after being accused of cowardice. June 25: Smith and his brother Hyrum voluntarily surrender to authorities at Carthage, Illinois, on charges of treason against the state.

June 27, 1844, approximately 5:00 PM: A mob of 150-200 men with blackened faces storms Carthage Jail. Hyrum Smith is shot and killed first. Joseph fires a smuggled pepper-box pistol at the attackers, wounding three. He then ran to the window and was hit by multiple gunshots. His last words, according to witnesses, were "O Lord, my God!" — possibly the beginning of a Masonic distress call. He fell from the second-story window and was dead at age 38.

📍 Carthage Jail, Carthage, Illinois
Key figures: Joseph Smith, Hyrum Smith, John Taylor (survived, wounded), Willard Richards (survived, unhurt)
1844 – 1847 — The Succession Crisis

Who Leads the Church Now?

Smith's death created a massive power vacuum. He had never clearly designated a successor. Multiple factions claimed authority:

Brigham Young & the Twelve Apostles: Won the majority of followers. Young led them west to the Salt Lake Valley in 1847, founding what became the main LDS Church (headquartered in Salt Lake City today). Sidney Rigdon: Claimed authority as remaining member of the First Presidency; formed a small church that quickly dissolved. James Strang: Produced a letter allegedly from Smith appointing him; attracted a following and established a colony on Beaver Island, Michigan (was later assassinated). Emma Smith & the Reorganized Church: Emma refused to go west and later supported her son Joseph Smith III in founding the Reorganized Church of Jesus Christ of Latter Day Saints (now Community of Christ, ~250,000 members today), which rejected polygamy and Brigham Young's authority.

📍 Nauvoo, IL → Salt Lake Valley, Utah Territory
Key figures: Brigham Young, Sidney Rigdon, James Strang, Joseph Smith III, Emma Smith

What This All Means — The Big Takeaways
🌟

The Scale is Undeniable

Love him or dismiss him, Joseph Smith built a global religious movement from nothing in 14 years. Today, the LDS Church has 17+ million members, a financial empire estimated at $100+ billion in assets, the world's largest genealogical database, one of the largest private university systems (BYU), and a global missionary force of ~50,000 full-time missionaries at any given time. Whatever Joseph Smith was, he wasn't insignificant.

⚖

The Historical Record is Complicated

The LDS Church's own Gospel Topics Essays (published 2013-2015 on churchofjesuschrist.org) now acknowledge many things that were previously denied or downplayed: Smith's use of a seer stone in a hat, multiple accounts of the First Vision, the scope of polygamy, the translation of the Book of Abraham not matching the actual Egyptian papyri, and the racial ban on Black members holding the priesthood. The church itself is engaging with its complicated history more honestly than at any point in the past.

💡

The Witnesses Never Recanted

Here's the thing that makes skeptics pause: the Three Witnesses and Eight Witnesses to the golden plates were real people with real reputations. Many of them left the church, were excommunicated, had bitter falling-outs with Smith — and yet not a single one ever denied seeing the plates. Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer, and Martin Harris all had every incentive to recant. They didn't. Believers see this as proof; critics argue it could be a shared spiritual/psychological experience rather than a physical one.

⚠

Polygamy Changed Everything

Without polygamy, Joseph Smith probably doesn't die at Carthage. The Nauvoo Expositor wouldn't have existed. The press wouldn't have been destroyed. The treason charge wouldn't have been filed. The mob wouldn't have formed. Polygamy was the match that lit the fuse — and it remained the defining controversy of Mormonism for the next 50 years, leading to federal raids, a Supreme Court case (Reynolds v. United States, 1879), and ultimately the church's renunciation of the practice in 1890 to secure Utah statehood.

📖

The Real Story is More Interesting Than Either Side Tells It

The faithful narrative of a pure prophet called by God and the dismissive narrative of a conman making it all up are both too simple. The actual history involves a brilliant, charismatic, deeply flawed human being operating in a culture that was actively producing new religions. The Shakers, the Millerites, the Oneida Community, and dozens of other movements came from the same soil. Smith was the most successful by far. Whether that success was divine, genius, lucky, or some combination is the question that 200 years of debate hasn't settled — and probably won't.

Rough Stone Rolling

Richard Lushman Bushman's definitive biography of Joseph Smith. Written by a believing LDS historian using full access to church archives. The gold standard for understanding Smith with nuance.

Essential
02

No Man Knows My History

Fawn Brodie's 1945 biography — the first serious secular treatment of Smith's life. Brodie was excommunicated for writing it. Still remarkably insightful despite its age.

Classic
03

Gospel Topics Essays

The LDS Church's own essays addressing controversial topics — polygamy, the Book of Abraham, race, the First Vision accounts. Published on churchofjesuschrist.org. Start here for the church's modern position.

Official LDS
04

In Sacred Loneliness

Todd Compton's meticulous documentation of Joseph Smith's 33 plural wives. Each woman gets her own biography. Peer-reviewed, deeply researched, and hard to argue with.

Scholarly
05

The Joseph Smith Papers

A massive, ongoing project to digitize and publish every document written by or about Joseph Smith. Funded by the LDS Church. Available free at josephsmithpapers.org — primary sources for everything.

06

American Crucifixion

Alex Beam's account of the final months of Joseph Smith's life — the Nauvoo Expositor, the arrest, and Carthage. Reads like a thriller. Great entry point for someone who just wants the climax of the story.
